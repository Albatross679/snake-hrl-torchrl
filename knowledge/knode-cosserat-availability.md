# KNODE-Cosserat: Practical Availability and Usability Assessment

Research into whether KNODE-Cosserat (arXiv:2408.07776, Jiahao et al. 2024) is usable as a
learned forward dynamics model for our 20-segment snake robot with RFT friction.

**URL:** https://github.com/hsiehScalAR/KNODE-Cosserat

**Stats:** 12 stars, 2 forks, 15 commits, last updated August 9, 2024

## 2. Dependencies

Inferred from imports in source code (no requirements file exists):

- **Python:** No version specified; standard Python 3.x
- **PyTorch:** Core dependency for training (torch, torch.nn)
- **NumPy:** Physics computations
- **SciPy:** Optimization (shooting method solver)
- **PyElastica:** NOT used at all. The Cosserat rod model is custom-implemented from scratch.
- **torchdiffeq:** NOT used. Despite the "Neural ODE" name, this is NOT a standard Neural ODE
  with adaptive-step ODE solvers. It uses BDF2 time-stepping with a shooting method.

## 3. Does It Use PyElastica?

**No.** The entire Cosserat rod model is implemented from scratch in ~300 lines of
NumPy/PyTorch. It implements the Cosserat rod PDE directly:
- Spatial discretization into N=10 segments
- BDF2 (Backward Differentiation Formula, order 2) for temporal integration
- Shooting method for the spatial BVP at each time step
- Euler or RK4 for spatial integration along the rod

This is a completely independent Cosserat implementation, unrelated to PyElastica.

## 4. Architecture

**NOT a standard Neural ODE.** Despite the paper title, the architecture is:

- A simple feedforward MLP that provides **additive corrections** to the physics-based
  spatial derivatives at each discretization point
- Architecture: `Linear(28→512) → ELU → Linear(512→25)`
- Total parameters: ~28×512 + 512 + 512×25 + 25 = **27,433 parameters**
- Input (28 dims without history): current state y (19) + constitutive vars z (6) + tendon forces (3)
- Input (53 dims with history): adds yh (19) + zh (6) from previous time step
- Output (25 dims): corrections to state derivatives ys (19) + constitutive corrections z (6)
- Weight initialization: non-negative normal (mean=0.01, std=0.01) with clamping

The neural network is called at every spatial node during the shooting method integration.
It corrects the spatial ODE, not the temporal ODE. This is a key distinction.

## 5. Training

- **Optimizer:** Adam, lr=1e-2, with ReduceLROnPlateau (patience=80, factor=0.5)
- **Loss:** MSE on position + quaternion (as Euler angles) + velocity at keypoints (indices 2, 6, 9)
- **Training data:** 30 time steps (1.5 seconds) of rod trajectories
- **Training trajectories:** Sine wave tendon tensions with various periods
- **Epochs:** Default 1000
- **Evaluation metric:** Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) distance on 100-step (5-second) rollouts
- **Weight constraint:** Non-negative weights enforced via clamping after each update

Data is generated by running the physics simulator with known tendon tension profiles,
or from real-world motion capture (Vicon) of the physical robot.

## 6. What Physics Does It Handle?

### Handled
- **Gravity:** Yes (rhoAg term in dynamics)
- **Material damping:** Yes (Bse, Bbt damping matrices for bending/torsion)
- **Square-law drag:** Yes (C * q * |q| term — velocity-dependent quadratic drag)
- **Internal forces and moments:** Yes (full Cosserat rod constitutive law)
- **Rest configuration:** Yes (vstar = [0,0,1] for straight rod)

### NOT Handled
- **Rest curvature actuation (setting rest_kappa):** No. Actuation is tendon-based only.
  Tendons apply point forces along the rod, not curvature commands.
- **Resistive Force Theory (RFT) friction:** No. Uses simple quadratic drag, not
  anisotropic tangential/normal friction coefficients.
- **Contact/collision:** No. Frictionless, no self-contact.
- **Time-varying serpenoid wave inputs:** No. Inputs are tendon tensions (4 scalar values),
  not spatially-varying curvature commands.

## 7. What Robot Was It Tested On?

A **tendon-driven continuum arm** (NOT a snake robot):
- Length: 0.635 m, diameter: 6.35 mm Delrin centerbone
- 4 tendons at 90-degree intervals, offset 0.02 m from center
- 4 DC motors (Seeed Studio JGA25-371) with reel mechanisms
- Custom load cells for tendon tension measurement
- Fixed at one end (cantilever), free at the other
- Operates in 3D space under gravity

This is fundamentally different from our snake robot:
- Single cantilever arm vs. free-moving snake on a surface
- Tendon actuation vs. curvature (rest_kappa) actuation
- 10 segments vs. 20 segments
- No locomotion, no ground contact, no friction

## 8. Can It Be Used as a Forward Dynamics Model?

**In principle, yes, but with major caveats.**

The model does function as state + action → next_state: given the current rod state and
tendon tensions, BDF2 time-stepping produces the next state. The neural network corrects
the spatial derivatives to improve accuracy.

However, the "forward model" involves solving a **boundary value problem** at each time step
via the shooting method (iterative optimization to find boundary conditions that satisfy
tip force/moment constraints). This is:
1. Computationally expensive (multiple ODE integrations per time step)
2. Not differentiable end-to-end through the shooting method solver
3. Not suitable for real-time RL inner loops

## 9. Multi-Step Rollout Accuracy

From the paper (Table III, simulation results):
- **Sine control inputs:** 89-100% DTW reduction, 91-100% MSE reduction vs. uncorrected physics
- **Step control inputs:** 65-83% DTW reduction, 48-98% MSE reduction
- **Evaluation horizon:** 100 time steps (5 seconds) — significantly longer than 30-step training

Real-world results (Table IV):
- **DTW improvement:** 19-81% across various control conditions
- **Position/rotation MSE improvement:** 50-83%
- Average improvement: 58.7% in real-world experiments

The model generalizes reasonably to unseen sine frequencies but degrades on step inputs
(which differ substantially from sinusoidal training data).

## 10. Adaptation Difficulty for Our Problem

**Very high. Essentially a rewrite.** Here's what would need to change:

| Aspect | KNODE-Cosserat | Our Problem | Gap |
|--------|---------------|-------------|-----|
| Actuation | 4 tendon tensions | 5D: amp, freq, wave_num, direction, phase | Complete redesign |
| Segments | 10 | 20 | Rescale, double computation |
| Boundary conditions | Cantilever (fixed base) | Free body on surface | Fundamental change |
| Friction model | Quadratic drag | Anisotropic RFT (c_t, c_n) | Must implement |
| Ground contact | None (hanging arm) | Full ground contact | Must implement |
| Spatial actuation | Uniform tendons | Spatially-varying curvature wave | Complete redesign |
| Time integration | BDF2 + shooting method | Needs to match PyElastica | Different solver |
| State representation | 19D per node (p,h,n,m,q,w) | Position + directors | Different format |
| Data source | Physical robot / custom sim | PyElastica simulation | Different simulator |

### What could be reused
- The general concept: physics model + neural correction network
- The loss function structure (MSE on keypoints)
- The training loop pattern (generate trajectories, train on short windows, evaluate on long rollouts)

### What must be built from scratch
- RFT friction model in the physics backbone
- Free-body (non-cantilever) boundary conditions
- Curvature-based actuation instead of tendon actuation
- Interface to PyElastica state format
- Differentiable forward model (current shooting method is not suitable for RL)

## 12. Bottom Line Assessment

**KNODE-Cosserat is not practically usable for our snake robot problem.** Specifically:

1. **Wrong actuation model:** Tendon tensions vs. curvature commands — cannot be adapted without rewriting the entire physics backbone.
2. **Wrong robot topology:** Fixed-base cantilever arm vs. free-moving snake on a surface.
3. **No friction/contact model:** The most important physics for our problem (RFT) is absent.
4. **Not a real Neural ODE:** Despite the name, it's a BVP shooting method with neural corrections, not an ODE solver with neural dynamics. Not suitable as a differentiable simulator for RL.
5. **Abandoned repo:** 15 commits, last activity August 2024, no community, no maintenance.
6. **No package structure:** Raw scripts, no installable package, no documentation of dependencies.

### What IS worth taking from this work
The **conceptual approach** is sound and relevant:
- Use an imperfect physics model as a backbone
- Train a small neural network to correct the spatial derivatives
- Train on short trajectories, evaluate on long rollouts
- The correction network is tiny (~27K params) yet effective

For our problem, a better path would be:
- Use PyElastica as the physics backbone (already working)
- Train a neural surrogate that maps (state, action) → next_state directly
- Skip the BVP/shooting method entirely — learn the temporal dynamics as a black-box
- Consider the DD-PINN approach (44,000x speedup) as a more promising architecture
