3. Paper's Assumptions & How Our Implementation Departs

The paper builds on a chain of eight assumptions (plus structural prerequisites) that together guarantee the existence of an optimal policy, convergence of value iteration, the policy difference lemma, the majorization bound, and finally the closed-form MM-RKHS update. Below we list each assumption, what it requires, and precisely why our continuous-control implementation cannot satisfy it.

Assumption What the paper requires Status Why our implementation cannot match it
Assumption 1
Well-posedness		 The initial state distribution $\rho \in \mathcal{P}_{\mathsf{w}_\mathcal{S}}(\mathcal{S})$. The cost function $c \in \mathcal{Q}$ is non-negative and inf-compact on $\mathcal{K}$.

This ensures the Bellman operator is well-defined and the cost has finite sublevel sets, so minimizers exist. 		partial Our reward function (negative of cost) is a hand-designed shaped reward that is bounded and continuous, so the non-negativity and inf-compactness conditions are plausibly satisfied. However, we never verify that $c \in \mathcal{Q}$ (the structured space of quadratic-and-affine functions) — our reward is a nonlinear composition of distances, angles, and exponentials that does not lie in any analytically tractable $\mathcal{Q}$.
Assumption 2
Nonempty $\Pi_\mathrm{stable}$		 The set of stable policies $\Pi_\mathrm{stable} = \Pi_\mathrm{ss} \cup \Pi_\mathrm{decay}$ is nonempty, where $\Pi_\mathrm{ss} = \{\pi : \mathrm{spec}(\gamma \boldsymbol{P}_\pi) < 1\}$ (spectrally stable) and $\Pi_\mathrm{decay}$ are decaying cost policies.

Required for the value function $v_\pi$ to exist and lie in $\mathcal{F}_\mathcal{S}$ (Theorem 3.8). 		unverifiable In continuous MDPs with neural network policies, the transition operator $\boldsymbol{P}_\pi$ is infinite-dimensional. Computing its spectral radius $\mathrm{spec}(\gamma \boldsymbol{P}_\pi)$ is intractable — it would require the full transition kernel $P(s'|s,a)$, which is unknown (physics simulation is a black box). We cannot verify whether any policy in our parameterized family is spectrally stable.
Assumption 3
Compatibility of operators and linear spaces		 $\boldsymbol{P}\mathcal{V} \subset \mathcal{Q}$ and $\boldsymbol{\pi}\mathcal{Q} \subset \mathcal{V}$ for all $\pi \in \Pi$.

Ensures that applying the transition operator to value functions stays in the structured space $\mathcal{Q}$, and applying the policy operator to Q-functions stays in $\mathcal{V}$. This makes value iteration well-defined within the structured spaces. 		violated Our value function is a neural network $V_\phi : \mathbb{R}^{d_s} \to \mathbb{R}$, which does not belong to any analytically defined $\mathcal{V}$. Applying the (unknown) transition operator $\boldsymbol{P}$ to $V_\phi$ produces an arbitrary function of $(s,a)$ that has no reason to lie in a structured $\mathcal{Q}$. The closure properties that make the paper's operator-theoretic framework work require algebraic structure (e.g., quadratics closed under linear transformations in LQR) that neural networks do not provide.
Assumption 4
Structured selectors		 For every $q \in \mathcal{Q}$ such that $q$ is inf-compact, there exists $\pi \in \Pi$ such that $$\pi(s) \in \underset{p \in \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{K}(s))}{\arg\min} \int q(s,a)\, p(da).$$ This guarantees that the greedy policy with respect to any Q-function in $\mathcal{Q}$ exists and lies in the policy class $\Pi$. violated Our policy class $\Pi$ is the set of TanhNormal distributions parameterizable by a fixed-architecture MLP. This is a tiny subset of all stochastic policies. The greedy policy $\arg\min_p \int q\, dp$ for an arbitrary $q$ is generally not a TanhNormal distribution — it could be multimodal, have sharp cutoffs, or require an architecture the network cannot represent. The paper's finite MDP satisfies this trivially because $\Pi$ contains all measurable policies (Lemma 6.2).
Assumption 5
Closed under bounded increasing limits		 $\mathcal{V}$ is closed under bounded increasing limits: if $\{v_k\} \subset \mathcal{V}$ with $v_0 \le v_1 \le \cdots$ and $\sup_k \|v_k\|_{\mathcal{F}_\mathcal{S}} < \infty$, then $\lim_k v_k \in \mathcal{V}$.

Required for value iteration to converge to a limit within $\mathcal{V}$ (Theorem 4.1). 		not applicable We do not perform value iteration over a structured space $\mathcal{V}$. Our critic $V_\phi$ is trained by regressing onto GAE targets via MSE loss — a completely different procedure. The limit of a sequence of neural networks under SGD has no guaranteed relationship to $\mathcal{V}$. This assumption is specific to the paper's operator-theoretic value iteration proof and has no analog in our gradient-based framework.
Assumption 6
Continuity of transition kernel		 $P$ is continuous with respect to $\mathrm{IPM}_{\mathfrak{F}_\mathcal{V}}$: for every sequence $(s_n, a_n) \to (s,a)$ in $\mathcal{K}$, $$\lim_{n \to \infty} \mathrm{IPM}_{\mathfrak{F}_\mathcal{V}}(P(\cdot|s,a),\, P(\cdot|s_n, a_n)) = 0.$$ Ensures $\boldsymbol{P}v$ is a continuous function when $v \in \mathcal{V}$, needed for the Bellman operator to preserve structure. unverifiable Our transition kernel is a DisMech physics simulator — a black-box numerical PDE solver. We have no analytical expression for $P(s'|s,a)$ and cannot verify continuity in any IPM. Physics simulators can exhibit discontinuities (contact events, numerical solver failures, bifurcations), and the IPM continuity condition requires the specific generator set $\mathfrak{F}_\mathcal{V}$, which is undefined for neural network value functions.
Assumption 7
Bounded transition operator		 $\exists\, \kappa_{\boldsymbol{P}} > 0$ such that $\varrho_{\mathfrak{F}_\mathcal{Q}}(\boldsymbol{P}v) \le \kappa_{\boldsymbol{P}} \|v\|_{\mathcal{F}_\mathcal{S}}$ for all $v \in \mathcal{V}$.

Bounds how much the transition operator $\boldsymbol{P}$ can amplify the Minskowski functional of functions in $\mathcal{V}$. This constant $\kappa_{\boldsymbol{P}}$ directly enters the majorization bound (Lemma 5.8) and determines the $\beta$ coefficient in Eq 6.1. 		unverifiable Computing $\kappa_{\boldsymbol{P}}$ requires knowing the transition operator $\boldsymbol{P}$ analytically and bounding its effect on the structured space $\mathcal{V}$. With a black-box physics simulator, neither $\boldsymbol{P}$ nor $\mathcal{V}$ is available. The paper computes $\kappa_{\boldsymbol{P}} = 2(\|A\|_2^2 + \|B\|_2^2)\|Q\|_2$ for LQR using known system matrices — we have no equivalent. Our $\beta = 1.0$ is a hand-tuned constant with no theoretical justification from the majorization bound.
Assumption 8
Interchange of integral and minimization		 $\mathcal{Q}$ and $\Pi$ enjoy the interchange property (Def 6.1): for any inf-compact $q \in \mathcal{Q}$ and $\xi \in \mathcal{P}_{\mathsf{w}_\mathcal{S}}(\mathcal{S})$, $$\underset{\pi \in \Pi}{\arg\min} \int q(s, \pi(s))\, \xi(ds) \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \pi^*(s) \in \underset{a \in \mathcal{K}(s)}{\arg\min}\, q(s,a) \;\; \text{for } \xi\text{-a.e. } s.$$ Allows pushing the global policy optimization inside the integral to solve per-state. violated This property requires that the global optimum over policies decomposes into independent per-state optima. With a neural network policy $\pi_\theta$, optimizing $\theta$ affects the policy at all states simultaneously — the parameters are shared. There is no way to set $\pi_\theta(\cdot|s_1)$ and $\pi_\theta(\cdot|s_2)$ independently because they share the same weights. The interchange property holds trivially for tabular policies (Lemma 6.2) but fails fundamentally for parameterized function approximators.
Finite MDP
§6.4 prerequisite		 $|\mathcal{S}| = n$, $|\mathcal{A}| = m$ both finite. $\mathsf{w}_\mathcal{S} \equiv 1$, $\mathsf{w}_\mathcal{K} \equiv 1$. Policies are vectors $\pi(s) \in \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{A})$ (the simplex in $m$ dimensions). RKHS kernel defined by PD matrices $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, $R \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$.

Required for the closed-form solution Eq (6.3): the normalizer $Z$ is a finite sum, the update is a vector operation, and $\mathrm{IPM}^2$ is a quadratic form $p^\top R p$. 		violated Our MDP has continuous $\mathcal{S} = \mathbb{R}^{d_s}$ and continuous $\mathcal{A} = [-1,1]^{d_a}$. The normalizer $Z$ becomes an intractable integral. The RKHS kernel matrix $R \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ cannot exist when $m = |\mathcal{A}| = \infty$ (uncountable action space). The quadratic form $p^\top R p$ has no finite-dimensional analog. Policies cannot be stored as probability vectors — they must be parameterized as distributions (TanhNormal).
Known model
§6.5 prerequisite		 The deterministic algorithm requires exact transition kernel $P(s'|s,a)$ and cost $c(s,a)$ to compute exact advantages $A_{\pi_k}(s,a)$. The simulation-based variant (Remark 6.3) approximates $\langle \pi' q_k, \sigma^*_{\pi_k} \rho \rangle$ via trajectory sampling with geometric episode lengths. violated Our environment is a black-box DisMech physics simulator. We have no access to $P(s'|s,a)$ and cannot compute exact $A_{\pi_k}(s,a)$. We estimate advantages via GAE($\lambda\!=\!0.95$) using a learned critic $V_\phi$, introducing both bias (critic approximation error, $\mathbb{E}[\hat{A}] \ne A$) and variance (finite trajectory samples, stochastic minibatching).
Summary: Of the 8 formal assumptions plus 2 structural prerequisites, our implementation can partially satisfy only Assumption 1 (well-posed cost). Assumptions 2, 6, and 7 are unverifiable due to the black-box physics simulator. Assumptions 3, 4, 5, and 8 are violated by the use of neural network function approximation. The finite MDP and known model prerequisites are violated by the continuous state-action space. These violations are not implementation shortcuts — they are fundamental incompatibilities between the paper's operator-theoretic framework and the deep RL setting.

6. Why Exact Replication Is Infeasible

Barrier Explanation
Intractable normalizer $Z$ The finite sum $Z = \sum_a \pi_k(a|s) \exp(\cdot)$ becomes $$Z = \int \pi_\theta(a|s)\, \exp\!\Big(\!-\eta\big(A(s,a) - \beta\, \mu_k(a|s)\big)\Big)\, da$$ in continuous spaces. This integral has no closed form for TanhNormal $\times$ exp(nonlinear). Numerical quadrature scales as $O(B^{d_a})$ — with $d_a = 10$, even $B=10$ gives $10^{10}$ evaluations per state.
Policy family not closed The update $\pi_{k+1}(a) \propto \pi_k(a) \cdot \exp(f(a))$ for arbitrary $f(a)$ generally produces a distribution that is not TanhNormal. The parametric family is not closed under the multiplicative update. Projection back (via moment matching or KL minimization) introduces additional approximation error.
$R_a^\top \pi_k(s)$ requires integration The continuous analog is the kernel mean embedding: $$\mu_k(a|s) = \int k(a, a')\, \pi_\theta(a'|s)\, da'$$ For RBF kernel $\times$ TanhNormal, no closed form exists. Must estimate via Monte Carlo: $\hat{\mu}_k(a|s) = \frac{1}{P}\sum_j k(a, a_j)$, $a_j \sim \pi_\mathrm{old}(\cdot|s)$.
No exact advantage Continuous environments do not provide a model ($P$, $c$). We can only estimate $A(s,a)$ from sampled trajectories using a learned critic and GAE. This introduces both bias (critic error, $\mathbb{E}[\hat{A}] \ne A$) and variance (finite samples).
Per-state optimization impossible With continuous $\mathcal{S}$, we cannot solve Eq 6.2 independently for each $s$. There are uncountably many states; we must generalize via function approximation ($\theta$), trading per-state optimality for generalization.
Convexity lost The paper's objective is strictly convex in $p$ on $\mathcal{P}(\mathcal{A})$. When parameterized as $\theta \mapsto \mathrm{TanhNormal}(\mu_\theta, \sigma_\theta)$, composition with the neural network makes the loss non-convex in $\theta$. The unique global minimum guarantee no longer holds.

7. What Our Implementation Preserves

Despite the differences above, our MMRKHSTrainer preserves the theoretical structure of the paper's objective (Eq 6.2). The three-term decomposition — surrogate advantage, MMD trust region penalty, and mirror descent KL regularizer — is faithfully maintained.

8. Verdict

Classification: Faithful Adaptation, Not Exact Replication

Our MMRKHSTrainer implements the same optimization objective as Gupta & Mahajan's Eq (6.2), adapted for continuous control with neural networks. The six barriers in Section 6 make exact replication of the closed-form update (Eq 6.3) mathematically impossible in our problem setting. The gradient-based approach we use is the natural and standard way to operationalize the theoretical framework for deep RL.

This is directly analogous to how PPO relates to the original TRPO theory: the theory proves convergence for exact policy updates; the practical algorithm uses clipped surrogate objectives with SGD. Our MM-RKHS implementation stands in the same relationship to Gupta & Mahajan's Eq 6.3 as PPO does to Schulman et al.'s TRPO theorem.
