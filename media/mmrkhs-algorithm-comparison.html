3. Paper's Requirements vs Our Implementation: Assumptions & Mechanisms

The paper's closed-form MM-RKHS update (Eq 6.3) rests on a chain of eight formal assumptions, two structural prerequisites, and six algorithmic mechanisms that are only tractable in finite MDPs. This section unifies all of these into a single reference, organized by the root cause that prevents replication. Each row states what the paper demands, what it enables in the algorithm, and why our continuous-control deep RL setting cannot satisfy it.

3.1 Formal Assumptions (Assumptions 1–8)

These assumptions are stated explicitly in the paper and are required for the theoretical results (existence of optimal policy, value iteration convergence, policy difference lemma, majorization bound) that lead to Eq 6.2–6.3.

Assumption What the paper requires & what it enables Status Why we cannot satisfy it
Assumption 1
Well-posedness		 The initial state distribution $\rho \in \mathcal{P}_{\mathsf{w}_\mathcal{S}}(\mathcal{S})$. The cost function $c \in \mathcal{Q}$ is non-negative and inf-compact on $\mathcal{K}$.

This ensures the Bellman operator is well-defined and the cost has finite sublevel sets, so minimizers exist. 		partial Our reward function (negative of cost) is a hand-designed shaped reward that is bounded and continuous, so the non-negativity and inf-compactness conditions are plausibly satisfied. However, we never verify that $c \in \mathcal{Q}$ (the structured space of quadratic-and-affine functions) — our reward is a nonlinear composition of distances, angles, and exponentials that does not lie in any analytically tractable $\mathcal{Q}$.
Assumption 2
Nonempty $\Pi_\mathrm{stable}$		 The set of stable policies $\Pi_\mathrm{stable} = \Pi_\mathrm{ss} \cup \Pi_\mathrm{decay}$ is nonempty, where $\Pi_\mathrm{ss} = \{\pi : \mathrm{spec}(\gamma \boldsymbol{P}_\pi) < 1\}$ (spectrally stable) and $\Pi_\mathrm{decay}$ are decaying cost policies.

Required for the value function $v_\pi$ to exist and lie in $\mathcal{F}_\mathcal{S}$ (Theorem 3.8). 		unverifiable In continuous MDPs with neural network policies, the transition operator $\boldsymbol{P}_\pi$ is infinite-dimensional. Computing its spectral radius $\mathrm{spec}(\gamma \boldsymbol{P}_\pi)$ is intractable — it would require the full transition kernel $P(s'|s,a)$, which is unknown (physics simulation is a black box). We cannot verify whether any policy in our parameterized family is spectrally stable.
Assumption 3
Compatibility of operators and linear spaces		 $\boldsymbol{P}\mathcal{V} \subset \mathcal{Q}$ and $\boldsymbol{\pi}\mathcal{Q} \subset \mathcal{V}$ for all $\pi \in \Pi$.

Ensures that applying the transition operator to value functions stays in the structured space $\mathcal{Q}$, and applying the policy operator to Q-functions stays in $\mathcal{V}$. This makes value iteration well-defined within the structured spaces. 		violated Our value function is a neural network $V_\phi : \mathbb{R}^{d_s} \to \mathbb{R}$, which does not belong to any analytically defined $\mathcal{V}$. Applying the (unknown) transition operator $\boldsymbol{P}$ to $V_\phi$ produces an arbitrary function of $(s,a)$ that has no reason to lie in a structured $\mathcal{Q}$. The closure properties that make the paper's operator-theoretic framework work require algebraic structure (e.g., quadratics closed under linear transformations in LQR) that neural networks do not provide.
Assumption 4
Structured selectors		 For every $q \in \mathcal{Q}$ such that $q$ is inf-compact, there exists $\pi \in \Pi$ such that $$\pi(s) \in \underset{p \in \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{K}(s))}{\arg\min} \int q(s,a)\, p(da).$$ This guarantees that the greedy policy with respect to any Q-function in $\mathcal{Q}$ exists and lies in the policy class $\Pi$. violated Our policy class $\Pi$ is the set of TanhNormal distributions parameterizable by a fixed-architecture MLP. This is a tiny subset of all stochastic policies. The greedy policy $\arg\min_p \int q\, dp$ for an arbitrary $q$ is generally not a TanhNormal distribution — it could be multimodal, have sharp cutoffs, or require an architecture the network cannot represent. The paper's finite MDP satisfies this trivially because $\Pi$ contains all measurable policies (Lemma 6.2).
Assumption 5
Closed under bounded increasing limits		 $\mathcal{V}$ is closed under bounded increasing limits: if $\{v_k\} \subset \mathcal{V}$ with $v_0 \le v_1 \le \cdots$ and $\sup_k \|v_k\|_{\mathcal{F}_\mathcal{S}} < \infty$, then $\lim_k v_k \in \mathcal{V}$.

Required for value iteration to converge to a limit within $\mathcal{V}$ (Theorem 4.1). 		not applicable We do not perform value iteration over a structured space $\mathcal{V}$. Our critic $V_\phi$ is trained by regressing onto GAE targets via MSE loss — a completely different procedure. The limit of a sequence of neural networks under SGD has no guaranteed relationship to $\mathcal{V}$. This assumption is specific to the paper's operator-theoretic value iteration proof and has no analog in our gradient-based framework.
Assumption 6
Continuity of transition kernel		 $P$ is continuous with respect to $\mathrm{IPM}_{\mathfrak{F}_\mathcal{V}}$: for every sequence $(s_n, a_n) \to (s,a)$ in $\mathcal{K}$, $$\lim_{n \to \infty} \mathrm{IPM}_{\mathfrak{F}_\mathcal{V}}(P(\cdot|s,a),\, P(\cdot|s_n, a_n)) = 0.$$ Ensures $\boldsymbol{P}v$ is a continuous function when $v \in \mathcal{V}$, needed for the Bellman operator to preserve structure. unverifiable Our transition kernel is a DisMech physics simulator — a black-box numerical PDE solver. We have no analytical expression for $P(s'|s,a)$ and cannot verify continuity in any IPM. Physics simulators can exhibit discontinuities (contact events, numerical solver failures, bifurcations), and the IPM continuity condition requires the specific generator set $\mathfrak{F}_\mathcal{V}$, which is undefined for neural network value functions.
Assumption 7
Bounded transition operator		 $\exists\, \kappa_{\boldsymbol{P}} > 0$ such that $\varrho_{\mathfrak{F}_\mathcal{Q}}(\boldsymbol{P}v) \le \kappa_{\boldsymbol{P}} \|v\|_{\mathcal{F}_\mathcal{S}}$ for all $v \in \mathcal{V}$.

Bounds how much the transition operator $\boldsymbol{P}$ can amplify the Minskowski functional of functions in $\mathcal{V}$. This constant $\kappa_{\boldsymbol{P}}$ directly enters the majorization bound (Lemma 5.8) and determines the $\beta$ coefficient in Eq 6.1. 		unverifiable Computing $\kappa_{\boldsymbol{P}}$ requires knowing the transition operator $\boldsymbol{P}$ analytically and bounding its effect on the structured space $\mathcal{V}$. With a black-box physics simulator, neither $\boldsymbol{P}$ nor $\mathcal{V}$ is available. The paper computes $\kappa_{\boldsymbol{P}} = 2(\|A\|_2^2 + \|B\|_2^2)\|Q\|_2$ for LQR using known system matrices — we have no equivalent. Our $\beta = 1.0$ is a hand-tuned constant with no theoretical justification from the majorization bound.
Assumption 8
Interchange of integral and minimization		 $\mathcal{Q}$ and $\Pi$ enjoy the interchange property (Def 6.1): for any inf-compact $q \in \mathcal{Q}$ and $\xi \in \mathcal{P}_{\mathsf{w}_\mathcal{S}}(\mathcal{S})$, $$\underset{\pi \in \Pi}{\arg\min} \int q(s, \pi(s))\, \xi(ds) \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \pi^*(s) \in \underset{a \in \mathcal{K}(s)}{\arg\min}\, q(s,a) \;\; \text{for } \xi\text{-a.e. } s.$$ Allows pushing the global policy optimization inside the integral to solve per-state. violated This property requires that the global optimum over policies decomposes into independent per-state optima. With a neural network policy $\pi_\theta$, optimizing $\theta$ affects the policy at all states simultaneously — the parameters are shared. There is no way to set $\pi_\theta(\cdot|s_1)$ and $\pi_\theta(\cdot|s_2)$ independently because they share the same weights. The interchange property holds trivially for tabular policies (Lemma 6.2) but fails fundamentally for parameterized function approximators.
Finite MDP
§6.4 prerequisite		 $|\mathcal{S}| = n$, $|\mathcal{A}| = m$ both finite. $\mathsf{w}_\mathcal{S} \equiv 1$, $\mathsf{w}_\mathcal{K} \equiv 1$. Policies are vectors $\pi(s) \in \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{A})$ (the simplex in $m$ dimensions). RKHS kernel defined by PD matrices $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, $R \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$.

Required for the closed-form solution Eq (6.3): the normalizer $Z$ is a finite sum, the update is a vector operation, and $\mathrm{IPM}^2$ is a quadratic form $p^\top R p$. 		violated Our MDP has continuous $\mathcal{S} = \mathbb{R}^{d_s}$ and continuous $\mathcal{A} = [-1,1]^{d_a}$. The normalizer $Z$ becomes an intractable integral. The RKHS kernel matrix $R \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ cannot exist when $m = |\mathcal{A}| = \infty$ (uncountable action space). The quadratic form $p^\top R p$ has no finite-dimensional analog. Policies cannot be stored as probability vectors — they must be parameterized as distributions (TanhNormal).
Known model
§6.5 prerequisite		 The deterministic algorithm requires exact transition kernel $P(s'|s,a)$ and cost $c(s,a)$ to compute exact advantages $A_{\pi_k}(s,a)$. The simulation-based variant (Remark 6.3) approximates $\langle \pi' q_k, \sigma^*_{\pi_k} \rho \rangle$ via trajectory sampling with geometric episode lengths. violated Our environment is a black-box DisMech physics simulator. We have no access to $P(s'|s,a)$ and cannot compute exact $A_{\pi_k}(s,a)$. We estimate advantages via GAE($\lambda\!=\!0.95$) using a learned critic $V_\phi$, introducing both bias (critic approximation error, $\mathbb{E}[\hat{A}] \ne A$) and variance (finite trajectory samples, stochastic minibatching).

3.3 Algorithmic Mechanisms (Eq 6.2–6.3)

The formal assumptions and prerequisites together make six specific algorithmic mechanisms tractable. Each is required for the closed-form MM-RKHS update. Below, each row states what the paper demands, and why our setting cannot replicate it.

Mechanism What the paper demands What we cannot replicate & why
Normalizing constant $Z$ Requirement: Compute the partition function $$Z(\pi_k) = \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} [\pi_k(s)]_a \exp\!\Big(\!-\eta_k\big(A_{\pi_k}(s,a) - \beta_k R_a^\top \pi_k(s)\big)\Big)$$ Why needed: Normalizes the closed-form update (Eq 6.3) so that $\pi_{k+1}(s)$ is a valid probability distribution on the simplex $\mathcal{P}(\mathcal{A})$.

Paper's setting: A finite sum over $m = |\mathcal{A}|$ terms. $O(m)$ per state. 		Barrier: Intractable integral.

In continuous action spaces, the sum becomes: $$Z = \int_{\mathcal{A}} \pi_\theta(a|s)\, \exp\!\Big(\!-\eta\big(A(s,a) - \beta\,\mu_k(a|s)\big)\Big)\, da$$ Why no closed form: The integrand is $\mathrm{TanhNormal}(a;\mu_\theta,\sigma_\theta) \times \exp(\text{nonlinear function of } a)$. The TanhNormal density involves $\tanh^{-1}$ and Jacobian corrections; multiplied by an exponential of the advantage (itself a neural network output), the product has no analytical antiderivative.

Why quadrature fails: Numerical integration on a grid with $B$ bins per dimension costs $O(B^{d_a})$. With $d_a = 10$ action dimensions and $B = 10$, that is $10^{10}$ evaluations per state per iteration. A single minibatch of 1024 states would require ${\sim}10^{13}$ function evaluations.

Our workaround: No normalizer needed. The TanhNormal parametric family is self-normalizing. We optimize $\theta$ via gradient descent instead of computing $Z$ explicitly.
Closed-form multiplicative update Requirement: Apply Eq (6.3) directly: $$[\pi_{k+1}(s)]_a = \frac{1}{Z(\pi_k)} [\pi_k(s)]_a \exp\!\Big(\!-\eta_k\big(A_{\pi_k}(s,a) - \beta_k R_a^\top \pi_k(s)\big)\Big)$$ Why needed: This is the exact solution to the optimization problem in Eq (6.2). It achieves the global minimum of the strictly convex objective in a single step — no iterative optimization required.

Paper's setting: Write the new probability for each of the $m$ actions. One pass. Exact. 		Barrier: Parametric family not closed under multiplicative updates.

The update $\pi_{k+1}(a) \propto \pi_k(a) \cdot \exp(f(a))$ for arbitrary $f(a)$ generally produces a distribution that is not TanhNormal. Specifically:
  • Shape mismatch: TanhNormal is unimodal and symmetric about $\mu$. Multiplying by $\exp(f(a))$ where $f$ has multiple peaks or asymmetric curvature creates multimodal or skewed distributions outside the TanhNormal family.
  • No projection: To stay in the TanhNormal family, one would need to project back (e.g., minimize $D_\mathrm{KL}(\pi_\mathrm{exact} \| \pi_\mathrm{TanhNormal})$), which itself requires the intractable $Z$ and introduces additional approximation error.
  • Parameter coupling: Even if we could project, the neural network maps $s \mapsto (\mu_\theta(s), \sigma_\theta(s))$, so changing the policy at one state via $\theta$ changes it at all states simultaneously.
Our workaround: Gradient descent on the combined loss $L(\theta)$ over minibatch epochs. Iterative, approximate, but compatible with any differentiable parametric family.
Kernel coupling $R_a^\top \pi_k(s)$ Requirement: Compute the inner product $$R_a^\top \pi_k(s) = \sum_{a'=1}^{m} R_{a,a'}\, \pi_k(a'|s)$$ and embed it inside the exponent of Eq (6.3) alongside the advantage $A(s,a)$.

Why needed: This term is the kernel mean embedding that couples the IPM trust region penalty to each individual action. It modifies the effective advantage per action, making the trust region action-aware rather than a batch-level scalar.

Paper's setting: A matrix-vector product. $R \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ is a known PD matrix. $O(m)$ per action per state. 		Barrier: Requires integration over continuous policy.

The continuous analog is the kernel mean embedding: $$\mu_k(a|s) = \int k(a, a')\, \pi_\theta(a'|s)\, da'$$ Why no closed form: For our RBF kernel $k(a,a') = \exp(-\|a-a'\|^2/2\sigma^2)$ and TanhNormal policy, this integral has no analytical solution. The RBF kernel is Gaussian in action space, but the TanhNormal density is not Gaussian (it has the $\tanh$ squashing and Jacobian). The convolution of these two functions does not simplify.

Monte Carlo alternative: We could estimate $\hat{\mu}_k(a|s) = \frac{1}{P}\sum_{j=1}^P k(a, a_j)$ with $a_j \sim \pi_\mathrm{old}(\cdot|s)$, but this gives a noisy per-action estimate, not the exact quantity. With $P=16$ samples, the variance is high.

Our workaround: The $R_a^\top\pi_k(s)$ coupling is absent entirely. MMD is computed as a separate batch-level scalar penalty $\widehat{\mathrm{MMD}}^2$ and added to the loss. The per-action trust region structure is lost.
Exact advantage $A_{\pi_k}(s,a)$ Requirement: Compute the true advantage function $$A_{\pi_k}(s,a) = q_{\pi_k}(s,a) - v_{\pi_k}(s)$$ where $q_{\pi_k}(s,a) = c(s,a) + \gamma \sum_{s'} P(s'|s,a)\, v_{\pi_k}(s')$.

Why needed: The closed-form update (Eq 6.3) is derived under the assumption that $A_{\pi_k}$ is exact. Errors in the advantage estimate break the majorization bound that guarantees monotonic improvement.

Paper's setting: Deterministic version uses exact model. Simulation version (§6.5) samples trajectories but still applies the exact closed-form update. 		Barrier: Black-box environment, no model access.

Our DisMech physics simulator provides no access to $P(s'|s,a)$, $c(s,a)$, or $v_{\pi_k}(s)$.

Error sources in our estimate:
  • Critic approximation: $V_\phi(s) \approx v_{\pi_k}(s)$ with unknown error $\epsilon(s) = V_\phi(s) - v_{\pi_k}(s)$.
  • GAE bias-variance: $\hat{A}_t^\mathrm{GAE} = \sum_{\ell=0}^{\infty} (\gamma\lambda)^\ell \delta_{t+\ell}$ introduces bias proportional to $(1-\lambda)$ and variance proportional to trajectory length.
  • Finite samples: Advantages estimated from $N \cdot T$ transitions per iteration (e.g., 8192), not the full state space.
Our workaround: GAE($\lambda\!=\!0.95$) with learned critic $V_\phi$, normalized per-minibatch.
Per-state independent optimization Requirement: Solve Eq (6.2) independently for each state $s \in \mathcal{S}$: $$\pi_{k+1}(s) = \underset{p \in \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{A})}{\arg\min}\; [\text{objective}(s, p)]$$ Each state gets its own optimal $\pi_{k+1}(s)$ with no coupling to other states.

Why needed: Assumption 8 (interchange of integral and minimization) allows decomposing the global problem into independent per-state subproblems, each a small convex program on the $m$-dimensional simplex.

Paper's setting: $n = |\mathcal{S}|$ independent optimizations. $O(n \cdot m)$ total. 		Barrier: Shared parameters across all states.

Our policy $\pi_\theta$ is a single neural network with parameters $\theta$ shared across all states.
  • No independence: There is no way to set $\pi_\theta(\cdot|s_1)$ and $\pi_\theta(\cdot|s_2)$ to different targets independently. The function $s \mapsto (\mu_\theta(s), \sigma_\theta(s))$ is globally determined by $\theta$.
  • Conflicting gradients: A gradient step that improves the loss at states in one minibatch may worsen it at states in the next (the fundamental interference problem of function approximation in RL).
  • Continuous state space: Even without parameter sharing, there are uncountably many states — per-state optimization is impossible in principle.
Our workaround: Global optimization of $\theta$ via minibatch SGD. The neural network generalizes across states but cannot achieve per-state optimality.
Strict convexity and global optimum Requirement: The objective in Eq (6.2) is strictly convex in $p$ on the simplex $\mathcal{P}(\mathcal{A})$:
  • $A_{\pi_k}(s,\cdot)^\top p$ — linear in $p$ (convex).
  • $\mathrm{IPM}^2(\pi_k(s), p) = p^\top R\, p + \text{const}$ — quadratic with PD $R$ (strictly convex).
  • $D_\mathrm{KL}(p \| \pi_k(s))$ — strictly convex in $p$.
Why needed: Unique global minimum guaranteed. The closed-form (Eq 6.3) is that unique minimum. No local minima, no initialization dependence. 		Barrier: Neural network parameterization destroys convexity.

When the optimization variable changes from $p \in \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{A})$ to $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^d$:
  • Non-convex composition: Even if $L(p)$ is convex in $p$, the map $\theta \mapsto \pi_\theta$ involves nonlinear activations (ReLU, tanh), making $L(\pi_\theta)$ non-convex in $\theta$.
  • Loss landscape: Saddle points, local minima, and flat regions. Adam may converge to any of these.
  • No monotonic improvement: The majorization bound guarantees $v_{\pi_{k+1}} \le v_{\pi_k}$. With SGD, this guarantee is lost — the policy can degrade between iterations.
Our workaround: Adam with gradient clipping (max_grad_norm=0.5), $K=10$ epochs, and LR scheduling. Heuristic stabilizers, not theoretical guarantees.
Summary: Of the 8 formal assumptions, Assumption 1 is partially satisfied (bounded reward). Assumptions 2, 6, 7 are unverifiable (black-box simulator). Assumptions 3, 4, 5, 8 are violated (neural network function approximation). Both structural prerequisites are violated (continuous spaces, unknown model). All 6 algorithmic mechanisms are unreplicable (intractable integrals, non-closed parametric family, shared parameters, non-convex optimization). These are fundamental incompatibilities between the operator-theoretic framework and deep RL, not implementation shortcuts.

7. Verdict

Classification: Faithful Adaptation, Not Exact Replication

Our MMRKHSTrainer implements the same optimization objective as Gupta & Mahajan's Eq (6.2), adapted for continuous control with neural networks. The six barriers in Section 6 make exact replication of the closed-form update (Eq 6.3) mathematically impossible in our problem setting. The gradient-based approach we use is the natural and standard way to operationalize the theoretical framework for deep RL.

This is directly analogous to how PPO relates to the original TRPO theory: the theory proves convergence for exact policy updates; the practical algorithm uses clipped surrogate objectives with SGD. Our MM-RKHS implementation stands in the same relationship to Gupta & Mahajan's Eq 6.3 as PPO does to Schulman et al.'s TRPO theorem.
