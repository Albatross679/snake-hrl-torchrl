\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

Reinforcement learning for soft and continuum robots is bottlenecked by the cost of first-principles simulation.
A snake robot modeled as a Cosserat rod requires resolving elastic body dynamics, anisotropic ground friction, and internally generated muscle moments at every simulation substep --- PyElastica \citep{Naughton2021} uses 500 Position Verlet substeps per control step, costing ${\sim}46$\,ms per environment step.
This report presents a neural surrogate $f_\theta \approx T$ that replaces the transition operator with a single GPU forward pass (${\sim}0.001$\,ms), targeting a four-to-five order-of-magnitude speedup for RL training.

The pipeline has three stages: (1)~parallel data collection from 16 independent PyElastica workers (\cref{sec:datacollection}), (2)~training a feedforward MLP surrogate on state-delta prediction with per-element CPG phase encoding (\cref{sec:surrogate}), and (3)~PPO training in the surrogate environment with a direct Elastica baseline for comparison (\cref{sec:rl}).
Two physics backends are investigated --- PyElastica (explicit symplectic) and DisMech (implicit backward Euler) --- providing complementary perspectives on surrogate fidelity (\cref{sec:physics}).

\noindent
Several common themes emerge from these studies.
CPG-based action spaces dominate snake robot RL, reducing the exploration burden relative to per-joint torque control \citep{Bing2019,Liu2021,Liu2023,Zheng2022,Jiang2024}.
Curriculum learning and domain randomization are standard techniques for bridging the sim-to-real gap \citep{Liu2023,Zheng2022}.
All approaches share the same simulation cost bottleneck that motivates this work: MuJoCo-based environments require ${\sim}1$\,ms per step, while Cosserat rod simulators such as PyElastica and DisMech cost $12$--$46$\,ms per step.
The neural surrogate strategy developed here can be viewed as a model-based RL method where the learned model is the full transition operator rather than a latent dynamics model, enabling direct policy transfer evaluation between surrogate and real simulator and providing a drop-in replacement for any of these physics backends.

\subsection{Cosserat Rod Formulation}
\label{sec:physics:formulation}

The Elastica backend solves a well-posed initial--boundary value problem for the Cosserat rod \citep{Till2019,Naughton2021}.
The system is fully specified by the tuple $\mathcal{S} = (\mathcal{L},\; \Omega,\; \text{BC},\; \text{IC},\; \boldsymbol{\theta})$:

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \small
  \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
  \caption{Well-posedness tuple for the Elastica Cosserat rod system.}
  \label{tab:system-tuple}
  \begin{tabular}{clp{8.5cm}}
    \toprule
    Component & Name & Specification \\
    \midrule
    $\mathcal{L}$ & Governing equations &
      Cosserat rod PDEs: conservation of linear and angular momentum (\cref{eq:pde-linear,eq:pde-angular}) with linear elastic constitutive law (\cref{eq:constitutive}) \\
    $\Omega$ & Domain &
      $s \in [0, L]$, $L = 0.5$\,m (arc length) $\times$ $t \in [0, T]$ (time) \\
    BC & Boundary conditions &
      Free--free: $\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{M} = \mathbf{0}$ at $s = 0$ and $s = L$ \\
    IC & Initial conditions &
      Straight rod at rest: $\mathbf{x}(s,0) = \mathbf{x}_0 + s\hat{\mathbf{e}}_x$,\; $\mathbf{v} = \boldsymbol{\kappa} = \boldsymbol{\omega} = \mathbf{0}$ \\
    $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ & Parameters &
      $E{=}10^5$\,Pa, $\nu{=}0.5$, $\rho{=}1200$\,kg/m$^3$, $R{=}0.02$\,m, $c_t{=}0.01$, $c_n{=}0.05$, $\gamma{=}0.002$ \\
    Control & CPG forcing &
      $\mathbf{a} = (A, f, k, \phi_0, b) \in \mathbb{R}^5$ enters as time-varying rest curvature $\boldsymbol{\kappa}_0(s,t)$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{PDE (continuous).}

\begin{subequations}
\label{eq:cosserat-pdes}
\begin{alignat}{2}
  \rho A \,\frac{\partial \mathbf{v}}{\partial t}
    &= \frac{\partial \mathbf{F}}{\partial s} + \mathbf{f}_{\text{ext}}
    &\qquad& \text{(linear momentum)}
  \label{eq:pde-linear} \\[4pt]
  \rho \mathbf{I} \,\frac{\partial \boldsymbol{\omega}}{\partial t}
    &= \frac{\partial \mathbf{M}}{\partial s}
      + \mathbf{m}_{\text{ext}}
      + \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}}{\partial s} \times \mathbf{F}
    &\qquad& \text{(angular momentum)}
  \label{eq:pde-angular}
\end{alignat}
\end{subequations}

\noindent
Constitutive closure:

\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{S}\,(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} - \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0), \quad
  \mathbf{M} = \mathbf{B}\,(\boldsymbol{\kappa} - \boldsymbol{\kappa}_0), \quad
  \mathbf{S} = \operatorname{diag}(GA, GA, EA), \quad
  \mathbf{B} = \operatorname{diag}(EI, EI, GJ)
  \label{eq:constitutive}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{ODE (semi-discrete).}
Spatial discretization on a staggered grid ($N{=}21$ nodes, $N_e{=}20$ elements) replaces the PDE with 124 coupled first-order ODEs:

\begin{equation}
  \dot{\mathbf{s}} = \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{s},\, \mathbf{a},\, t), \qquad
  \mathbf{s} \in \mathbb{R}^{124},\;\; \mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{R}^{5}
  \label{eq:ode-system}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Equation count per RL step.}
Each of the 124 state variables is advanced by one first-order ODE, giving 124 coupled equations.
The Position Verlet integrator evaluates the right-hand side $\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{a}, t)$ once per substep.
With $n_{\text{sub}} = 500$ substeps per RL action, a single RL step requires $500 \times 124 = 62{,}000$ scalar ODE evaluations, plus 500 curvature updates across 19 Voronoi nodes ($9{,}500$ trigonometric evaluations) and 500 friction force computations across 21 nodes ($10{,}500$ vector projections).
The total per-step computational budget is approximately $82{,}000$ scalar operations, or ${\sim}377{,}000$ FLOPs including the matrix--vector products and cross products within each evaluation.

\paragraph{Integrator (fully discrete).}
Position Verlet ($\Delta t = 0.001$\,s, $n_{\text{sub}} = 500$ substeps per RL step of $\Delta t_{\text{ctrl}} = 0.5$\,s) advances the ODE to produce the transition operator:

\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{s}_{t+1} = T(\mathbf{s}_t,\, \mathbf{a}_t)
    = \underbrace{V_{\Delta t}(\,\cdot\,;\, \boldsymbol{\kappa}^{\text{rest},499}) \circ \cdots \circ V_{\Delta t}(\,\cdot\,;\, \boldsymbol{\kappa}^{\text{rest},0})}_{500 \text{ substeps, each with distinct rest curvature}}(\mathbf{s}_t)
  \label{eq:transition-operator}
\end{equation}

\noindent
\Cref{alg:elastica-transition} expands this operator into its full computational form.
The 2D restriction ($z \equiv 0$) is exact for planar locomotion.

\subsection{CPG Control}
\label{sec:physics:cpg}

The Central Pattern Generator (CPG) drives locomotion by imposing a time-varying rest curvature on the rod.
The RL agent selects five CPG wave parameters per control step:

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \small
  \caption{Action vector $\mathbf{a}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{5}$: CPG wave parameters.}
  \label{tab:action-vector}
  \begin{tabular}{clrl}
    \toprule
    Symbol & Name & Range & Units \\
    \midrule
    $A$      & Amplitude    & $[0, 5]$      & rad\,m$^{-1}$ \\
    $f$      & Frequency    & $[0.5, 3.0]$  & Hz \\
    $k$      & Wave number  & $[0.5, 3.5]$  & wavelengths/body \\
    $\phi_0$ & Phase offset & $[0, 2\pi]$   & rad \\
    $b$      & Turn bias    & $[-2, 2]$     & rad\,m$^{-1}$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent
These parameters define the serpenoid rest curvature at each Voronoi node:
\begin{equation}
  \kappa_v^{\text{rest}}(t) = A \sin(2\pi k \cdot s_v + 2\pi f \cdot t + \phi_0) + b, \qquad v = 1, \ldots, 19.
  \label{eq:serpenoid}
\end{equation}
The action parameters are held fixed for one RL step ($\Delta t_{\text{ctrl}} = 0.5$\,s), but $\kappa_v^{\text{rest}}$ is recomputed at every substep because the temporal phase $2\pi f \cdot t$ advances by $\Delta\phi = 2\pi f \Delta t$ per substep.
Over one RL step, the phase sweeps through $2\pi f \cdot \Delta t_{\text{ctrl}}$ radians --- up to $3\pi$ radians at $f = 3$\,Hz.
The neural surrogate (\cref{sec:surrogate}) replaces the entire transition operator $T$ with a single forward pass, approximating the integrated effect of these 500 substeps.

DisMech applies curvature through the natural strain of discrete bend springs rather than the rest curvature array, and sets it once per RL step rather than per substep.
The curvature is applied as:
\begin{equation}
  \texttt{bend\_springs.nat\_strain}[j, 0] \;\gets\; \kappa_j^{\text{rest}},
  \quad j = 1, \ldots, 19.
  \label{eq:dismech-control-cpg}
\end{equation}
Because the implicit solver takes a single $\Delta t = 0.05$\,s step, the continuous curvature evolution that Elastica captures across 500 substeps is collapsed to a single snapshot at $t_{\text{step}}$ in DisMech.

$\mathbf{F}^{\text{ext}} \in \mathbb{R}^{21 \times 2}$ -- ext.\ forces \\
$\mathbf{m}^{\text{int}} \in \mathbb{R}^{20}$ -- int.\ torques \\
$\ddot{\mathbf{x}} \in \mathbb{R}^{21 \times 2}$ -- node accel. \\
$\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}}_z \in \mathbb{R}^{20}$ -- angular accel.
\end{minipage}%
\hfill
\vrule
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.68\linewidth}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State $\mathbf{s} \gets \mathbf{s}_t$
\For{$n = 0, 1, \ldots, n_{\text{sub}}{-}1$}
  \Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// Control input: PDE forcing term $\boldsymbol{\kappa}_0(s,t)$}
  \State $t^n \gets t_{\text{step}} + n \cdot \Delta t$
  \For{$v = 1, \ldots, 19$}
    \State $\kappa_v^{\text{rest}} \gets A \sin(2\pi k \cdot s_v + 2\pi f \cdot t^n + \phi_0) + b$
  \EndFor
  \Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// Integrator: Verlet half-step}
  \State $\mathbf{q}^{1/2} \gets \mathbf{q} + \tfrac{1}{2}\Delta t\;\dot{\mathbf{q}}$
  \Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// ODE right-hand side: discretized PDE}
  \Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// --- geometry}
  \State $\Delta\mathbf{x}_e \gets \mathbf{D}\,\mathbf{x}^{1/2}$
  \State $\ell_e \gets \|\Delta\mathbf{x}_e\|$
  \State $\mathbf{t}_e \gets \Delta\mathbf{x}_e / \ell_e$
  \State $\varepsilon_e \gets \ell_e / \ell_e^{\text{rest}}$
  \Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// --- shear--stretch: internal forces on nodes (translational)}
  \State $\boldsymbol{\sigma}_e \gets \mathbf{Q}_e^T(\Delta\mathbf{x}_e / \ell_e^{\text{rest}})$
  \State $\mathbf{n}_e^L \gets \mathbf{S}\,(\boldsymbol{\sigma}_e - \boldsymbol{\sigma}_e^{\text{rest}})$
  \State $\mathbf{F}^{\text{int}} \gets \mathbf{D}^T[\mathbf{Q}_e\,\mathbf{n}_e^L / \varepsilon_e]$
  \Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// --- bend--twist: internal torques on elements (rotational)}
  \State $\boldsymbol{\kappa}_v \gets \text{curvature from directors at Voronoi}$
  \State $\boldsymbol{\tau}_v^L \gets \mathbf{B}\,(\boldsymbol{\kappa}_v - \boldsymbol{\kappa}^{\text{rest}})$
  \State $\mathbf{m}^{\text{int}} \gets \mathbf{D}_v[\boldsymbol{\tau}_v^L / \hat{\varepsilon}_v^3]
    + \mathbf{A}_v[(\boldsymbol{\kappa}_v {\times} \boldsymbol{\tau}_v^L) \hat{\ell}_v / \hat{\varepsilon}_v^3]$
  \Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\hspace{\algorithmicindent}$\quad + (\mathbf{Q}_e\mathbf{t}_e) {\times} \mathbf{n}_e^L \ell_e^{\text{rest}}
    + \ldots$
  \Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// --- external forces on nodes ($\mathbb{R}^{21 \times 2}$)}
  \State $\mathbf{F}^{\text{ext}} \gets -c_t \mathbf{v}_{t} - c_n \mathbf{v}_{n} -\gamma\dot{\mathbf{x}} + \mathbf{m}\,\mathbf{g}^T$
  \Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// --- accelerations}
  \State $\ddot{\mathbf{x}} \gets (\mathbf{F}^{\text{int}} + \mathbf{F}^{\text{ext}}) \oslash \mathbf{m}\mathbf{1}^T$
  \State $\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}}_z \gets \mathbf{m}^{\text{int}} \oslash (\varepsilon_e \odot \mathbf{J})$
  \Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// Integrator: Verlet velocity update $+$ full-step}
  \State $\dot{\mathbf{q}} \gets \dot{\mathbf{q}} + \Delta t\;\ddot{\mathbf{q}}$
  \State $\mathbf{q} \gets \mathbf{q}^{1/2} + \tfrac{1}{2}\Delta t\;\dot{\mathbf{q}}$
\EndFor
\State \textbf{return} $\mathbf{s} = (\mathbf{q},\, \dot{\mathbf{q}})$
\end{algorithmic}
\vspace{4pt}
\noindent\rule{\linewidth}{0.4pt}
\vspace{2pt}
\textit{Cost:} ${\sim}754$ scalar ops/substep $\times$ 500 $= {\sim}377{,}000$ ops/step (${\sim}46$\,ms on CPU). \\
\textit{Key:} $\boldsymbol{\kappa}^{\text{rest}}$ changes every substep --- the CPG phase advances by $\Delta\phi = 2\pi f \Delta t$ per substep, accumulating $2\pi f \cdot \Delta t_{\text{ctrl}}$ radians over one RL step. The surrogate must learn the \textit{integrated effect} of this traveling wave, not a single static curvature.
\end{minipage}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Method of Lines and Explicit Integration}
\label{sec:physics:mol}

The Cosserat rod is governed by PDEs in arc length~$s$ and time~$t$, yet \cref{alg:elastica-transition} contains no iterative solver --- every line is a direct assignment.
This is possible because of a \textbf{two-stage reduction} that converts the continuous PDE into a deterministic arithmetic map.

\paragraph{Stage 1 --- spatial discretization (method of lines).}
All spatial derivatives $\partial/\partial s$ are replaced by sparse matrix--vector products on the staggered grid (\cref{sec:physics:grid}):
\begin{itemize}
  \item Force gradient: $\partial \mathbf{F}/\partial s \;\to\; \mathbf{D}^T \mathbf{F}_e$ \quad (bidiagonal, $O(N)$ ops).
  \item Strain from geometry: $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_e = \mathbf{Q}_e^T (\mathbf{x}_{e+1} - \mathbf{x}_e)/\ell_e^{\text{rest}}$ \quad (subtract, rotate).
  \item Constitutive law: $\mathbf{F}_e = \mathbf{S}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_e - \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_e^{\text{rest}})$ \quad (pointwise multiply).
\end{itemize}
After replacement, no spatial derivative remains.
The PDE in $(s,t)$ becomes the ODE system $\dot{\mathbf{s}} = \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{a}, t)$ (\cref{eq:ode-system}), where $\mathbf{g}$ is a \textbf{closed-form function} --- a chain of geometry $\to$ strains $\to$ constitutive law $\to$ forces $\to$ accelerations, each computed by explicit algebra.

\paragraph{Stage 2 --- explicit time integration.}
An explicit integrator advances the ODE by evaluating~$\mathbf{g}$ at the \emph{current} state and writing the result directly:
\[
  \mathbf{s}^{n+1} = \Phi(\mathbf{s}^n, \Delta t)
\]
The unknown $\mathbf{s}^{n+1}$ appears \emph{only on the left-hand side} --- there is no equation to solve.
Position Verlet evaluates $\mathbf{g}$ at a half-step configuration and updates positions and velocities in three sequential assignments.
The result: each substep~$V_{\Delta t}$ is a deterministic map $\mathbb{R}^{124} \to \mathbb{R}^{124}$ composed entirely of arithmetic.

\paragraph{Explicit vs.\ implicit integration.}
The defining distinction is whether the unknown state appears on the right-hand side.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \small
  \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
  \caption{Explicit vs.\ implicit update rules.}
  \label{tab:explicit-implicit-def}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Method} & \textbf{Update rule} & \textbf{Type} \\
    \midrule
    Forward Euler &
      $\mathbf{s}^{n+1} = \mathbf{s}^n + \Delta t\,\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{s}^n)$ &
      Explicit \\
    Backward Euler &
      $\mathbf{s}^{n+1} = \mathbf{s}^n + \Delta t\,\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{s}^{n+1})$ &

      \cref{alg:elastica-transition} &
      Explicit \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent
If $\mathbf{s}^{n+1}$ appears on the right-hand side, a Newton solve is required at every timestep (see \cref{sec:physics:dismech:implicit} for this approach in DisMech).

\paragraph{CFL stability constraint.}
Explicit methods are \textbf{conditionally stable}: the timestep must satisfy a Courant--Friedrichs--Lewy (CFL) bound set by the fastest wave mode (bending in the stiffest element):
\[
  \Delta t < C\,\sqrt{\frac{\rho A\,\Delta s^4}{EI}}
\]
With the project's rod parameters this gives $\Delta t_{\max} \approx 1.7 \times 10^{-3}$\,s.
PyElastica uses $\Delta t = 10^{-3}$\,s, safely below the limit, but this forces $n_{\text{sub}} = 500$ substeps per RL action ($\Delta t_{\text{ctrl}} = 0.5$\,s).
Implicit methods are unconditionally stable --- DisMech uses $\Delta t = 0.05$\,s (50$\times$ larger) at the cost of a Newton solve per step.
\Cref{tab:comprehensive-comparison} provides the full property comparison between the two backends.

\paragraph{Surrogate implications.}
The neural surrogate replaces the 500-step composition $T(\mathbf{s}_t, \mathbf{a}_t) = V_{\Delta t}^{500}(\mathbf{s}_t; \mathbf{a}_t)$ (\cref{eq:transition-operator})---a \textbf{deterministic sequential map}, not a PDE solver.
Each substep $V_{\Delta t}$ is a chain of explicit arithmetic (geometry, constitutive law, force balance, Verlet update); the computational cost arises from composing 500 such maps sequentially, with each depending on the output of the previous one.
The surrogate collapses this sequential composition into a single forward pass.
Because $T$ is a smooth, deterministic function with no iterative convergence involved, a feedforward network can approximate the composed map directly---in contrast to DisMech (\cref{sec:physics:dismech}), where the surrogate must additionally learn to bypass the Newton solver's convergence behavior.\looseness=-1

\subsection{DisMech Backend}
\label{sec:physics:dismech}

The second physics backend uses Discrete Elastic Rods (DER) via the DisMech simulator \citep{Bergou2008,Bergou2010}.
DisMech replaces PyElastica's explicit symplectic integration with implicit backward Euler, which is unconditionally stable and permits a single step of $\Delta t = 0.05$\,s per RL action --- eliminating the 500-substep inner loop.
\Cref{alg:dismech-transition} expands the resulting transition operator.

\paragraph{Primary unknowns.}
DER uses vertex positions and a material frame (Bishop frame).
At each internal vertex $i$ the discrete curvature binormal is:
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\kappa}_i
    = \frac{2\,\mathbf{e}_{i-1} \times \mathbf{e}_i}
           {|\mathbf{e}_{i-1}|\,|\mathbf{e}_i| + \mathbf{e}_{i-1} \cdot \mathbf{e}_i},
  \qquad
  \mathbf{e}_i = \mathbf{x}_{i+1} - \mathbf{x}_i.
  \label{eq:dismech-curvature-binormal}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Discrete bending energy.}
\begin{equation}
  E_{\text{bend},i}
    = \frac{1}{2\bar{\ell}_i}
      (\boldsymbol{\kappa}_i - \boldsymbol{\kappa}_i^{\text{rest}})^\top
      \mathbf{B}\,
      (\boldsymbol{\kappa}_i - \boldsymbol{\kappa}_i^{\text{rest}}),
  \label{eq:dismech-bend-energy}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\ell}_i$ is the Voronoi length (mean of adjacent edge lengths) and $\boldsymbol{\kappa}_i^{\text{rest}}$ is the rest curvature (control input).

\subsubsection{DisMech Algorithm: Discrete Elastic Rods}
\label{sec:physics:dismech:algorithm}

The DisMech transition operator solves a single implicit backward Euler step per RL action via Newton iteration.
\Cref{alg:dismech-transition} expands the full computational procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[H]\small
\caption{DisMech Transition Operator $T_{\text{DER}}(\mathbf{s}_t,\, \mathbf{a}_t,\, t_{\text{step}}) \to \mathbf{s}_{t+1}$}\label{alg:dismech-transition}
\vspace{2pt}
\noindent
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.28\linewidth}\scriptsize
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.08}
\textbf{Ensure} \\[1pt]
Next state $\mathbf{s}_{t+1} \in \mathbb{R}^{124}$

\vspace{4pt}
\textbf{Parameters} \\[1pt]
$\Delta t {=} 0.05$\,s -- timestep \\
$\epsilon_f {=} 10^{-4}$ -- force tol. \\
$\epsilon_d {=} 10^{-2}$ -- disp.\ tol. \\
$K {=} 25$ -- max Newton iter \\
$\mathbf{S}$ -- shear--stretch stiffness \\
$\mathbf{B}$ -- bending stiffness \\
$\mu$ -- friction coeff. \\
$m_i$ -- lumped mass at node $i$ \\
$\mathbf{g}$ -- gravity

\vspace{4pt}
\textbf{State} \\[1pt]
$\mathbf{q} \in \mathbb{R}^{63}$ -- positions: \\
\quad $x_i, y_i, z_i$ -- node coords \\
\quad ($z_i \equiv 0$ in 2D mode) \\
$\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^{63}$ -- velocities: \\
\quad $\dot{x}_i, \dot{y}_i, \dot{z}_i$ -- node vel. \\
$\mathbf{s} {=} (\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{v}) \in \mathbb{R}^{126}$

\vspace{4pt}
\textbf{CPG} (\cref{tab:action-vector}) \\[1pt]
$A, f, k, \phi_0, b \in \mathbb{R}$ -- from $\mathbf{a}_t$ \\
$s_j \in [0,1]$ -- arc-length \\
$t_{\text{step}} \in \mathbb{R}$ -- RL time

\vspace{4pt}
\textbf{Intermediates} \\[1pt]
$\boldsymbol{\kappa}^{\text{rest}} \in \mathbb{R}^{19}$ -- target curv. \\
$\mathbf{q}^{(k)} \in \mathbb{R}^{63}$ -- Newton iterate \\
$\mathbf{g} \in \mathbb{R}^{63}$ -- residual \\
$\mathbf{J} \in \mathbb{R}^{63 \times 63}$ -- Jacobian \\
\quad (sparse, banded) \\
$\delta\mathbf{q} \in \mathbb{R}^{63}$ -- correction \\
$\mathbf{e}_e \in \mathbb{R}^{20 \times 2}$ -- edge vectors \\
$\psi_e \in \mathbb{R}^{20}$ -- yaw angles \\
$\omega_{z,e} \in \mathbb{R}^{20}$ -- angular vel.
\end{minipage}%
\hfill
\vrule
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.68\linewidth}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State $\mathbf{s} \gets \mathbf{s}_t$
\Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// Control input: set curvature once per RL step}
\State $\omega \gets 2\pi f$
\For{$j = 1, \ldots, 19$}
  \State $\kappa_j^{\text{rest}} \gets A \sin(2\pi k \cdot s_j + \omega \cdot t_{\text{step}} + \phi_0) + b$
\EndFor
\Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// Apply to bend springs (DER control API)}
\For{$j = 1, \ldots, 19$}
  \State $\texttt{nat\_strain}[j, 0] \gets \kappa_j^{\text{rest}}$;\;
    $\texttt{nat\_strain}[j, 1] \gets 0$ \Comment{Planar}
\EndFor
\Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// Initial guess: explicit Euler predictor}
\State $\mathbf{q}^{(0)} \gets \mathbf{q} + \Delta t\;\mathbf{v}$
\Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// Newton iterations: solve $\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{q}_{n+1}) = \mathbf{0}$ (\cref{eq:dismech-residual})}
\For{$k = 0, 1, \ldots, K{-}1$}
  \Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// --- assemble residual}
  \State $\mathbf{F} \gets \mathbf{F}_{\text{elastic}}(\mathbf{q}^{(k)}) + \mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}}(\mathbf{q}^{(k)})$
  \State $\mathbf{g} \gets \mathbf{M}(\mathbf{q}^{(k)} - 2\mathbf{q} + \mathbf{q}_{\text{prev}}) - \Delta t^2\,\mathbf{F}$
  \Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// --- assemble Jacobian and solve}
  \State $\mathbf{J} \gets \partial\mathbf{g}/\partial\mathbf{q}\big|_{\mathbf{q}^{(k)}}$ \Comment{Sparse banded}
  \State $\delta\mathbf{q} \gets \mathbf{J}^{-1}\mathbf{g}$ \Comment{Direct factorization}
  \State $\mathbf{q}^{(k+1)} \gets \mathbf{q}^{(k)} - \delta\mathbf{q}$
  \Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// --- convergence check (\cref{tab:dismech-convergence})}
  \If{$\|\mathbf{g}\| < \epsilon_f$ \textbf{or} $\|\delta\mathbf{q}\| < \epsilon_d$}
    \State \textbf{break}
  \EndIf
\EndFor
\Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// Update velocity from converged position}
\State $\mathbf{v}' \gets (\mathbf{q}^{(K)} - \mathbf{q}) / \Delta t$
\Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}\textit{// Pack 3D state $\to$ 2D 124-dim (\cref{tab:dismech-state-extraction})}
\State $(x_i, y_i) \gets \mathbf{q}'_{3i{:}3i{+}2}$;\;
  $(\dot{x}_i, \dot{y}_i) \gets \mathbf{v}'_{3i{:}3i{+}2}$
  \Comment{Drop $z$}
\State $\mathbf{e}_e \gets \mathbf{D}\,\mathbf{x}'$;\;
  $\psi_e \gets \text{atan2}(e_{e,y},\, e_{e,x})$
\State $\omega_{z,e} \gets (\Delta x_e \Delta\dot{y}_e - \Delta y_e \Delta\dot{x}_e) / |\Delta\mathbf{x}_e|^2$
\State \textbf{return} $\mathbf{s}_{t+1} = (x, y, \dot{x}, \dot{y}, \psi, \omega_z) \in \mathbb{R}^{124}$
\end{algorithmic}
\vspace{4pt}
\noindent\rule{\linewidth}{0.4pt}
\vspace{2pt}
\textit{Cost:} 1 Newton solve with ${\sim}3$--$5$ iterations, ${\sim}12$\,ms on CPU (sparse direct factorization). No substep loop. \\
\textit{Key:} $\boldsymbol{\kappa}^{\text{rest}}$ is set \textit{once} per RL step --- the CPG wave is a single snapshot at $t_{\text{step}}$, unlike Elastica where the phase advances by $\Delta\phi = 2\pi f \Delta t_{\text{sub}}$ across 500 substeps. The surrogate must learn from this \textit{coarser temporal resolution}.
\end{minipage}
\end{algorithm}

% ---- Implicit time integration ----

\subsubsection{Implicit Time Integration}
\label{sec:physics:dismech:implicit}

\paragraph{Backward Euler formulation.}
Given positions $\mathbf{q}_n$ and velocities $\mathbf{v}_n$:
\begin{subequations}
\label{eq:dismech-backward-euler}
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{v}_{n+1} &= \mathbf{v}_n + \Delta t\,\mathbf{M}^{-1}\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{q}_{n+1},\, \mathbf{v}_{n+1}), \label{eq:dismech-be-vel} \\
  \mathbf{q}_{n+1} &= \mathbf{q}_n + \Delta t\,\mathbf{v}_{n+1}, \label{eq:dismech-be-pos}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
where $\mathbf{M}$ is the mass matrix and $\mathbf{F}$ includes all internal elastic forces and external forces.
$\mathbf{F}$ is evaluated at the unknown future state $(\mathbf{q}_{n+1}, \mathbf{v}_{n+1})$, making the system implicit.

\paragraph{Nonlinear residual.}
Substituting \cref{eq:dismech-be-pos} into \cref{eq:dismech-be-vel}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{q}_{n+1})
    \coloneqq \mathbf{M}\bigl(\mathbf{q}_{n+1} - 2\mathbf{q}_n + \mathbf{q}_{n-1}\bigr)
    - \Delta t^2\,\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{q}_{n+1})
    = \mathbf{0}.
  \label{eq:dismech-residual}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Newton solver.}
Each timestep solves \cref{eq:dismech-residual} iteratively:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{q}^{(k+1)} = \mathbf{q}^{(k)} - \mathbf{J}^{-1}(\mathbf{q}^{(k)})\,\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{q}^{(k)}),
  \qquad
  \mathbf{J} = \frac{\partial\mathbf{g}}{\partial\mathbf{q}}.
  \label{eq:dismech-newton}
\end{equation}

  \centering
  \small
  \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
  \caption{Newton solver convergence criteria.}
  \label{tab:dismech-convergence}
  \begin{tabular}{lll}
    \toprule
    Criterion & Condition & Default \\
    \midrule
    Force residual  & $\|\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{q}^{(k)})\| < \epsilon_f$ & $\epsilon_f = 10^{-4}$ \\
    Displacement    & $\|\delta\mathbf{q}\| < \epsilon_d$ & $\epsilon_d = 10^{-2}$ \\
    Iteration limit & $k < K$ & $K = 25$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent
Convergence is declared when either the force or displacement criterion is met.
The Jacobian $\mathbf{J}$ (tangent stiffness $+$ mass) is sparse; the linear system is solved via direct factorization.
The full computational procedure is given in \cref{alg:dismech-transition}.
The neural surrogate (\cref{sec:surrogate}) replaces $T_{\text{DER}}$ with a single forward pass, using the same architecture and training procedure as the Elastica surrogate.
The only adaptation is the state extraction layer (\cref{tab:dismech-state-extraction}) that packs DisMech's 3D vertex state into the shared 124-dimensional representation.

\subsection{Elastica Data Collection Pipeline}
\label{sec:datacollection:elastica}

Training data for the surrogate model is generated by running 16 independent worker processes, each maintaining its own PyElastica Cosserat rod simulation instance.
Actions are sampled from the 5-dimensional CPG action space using a Sobol quasi-random sequence, which distributes points more uniformly across all dimensions than pseudo-random sampling.
To populate diverse regions of the state space, 30\% of episodes begin with a state perturbation: random offsets are applied to node positions, and angular velocity noise is injected into the rod elements with $\sigma_\omega = 1.5$\,rad/s (see \cref{sec:discussion:pipeline} for the rationale behind this value).
Within each episode, 50\% of actions are drawn uniformly at random and the remaining 50\% follow the Sobol sequence.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Parallel data collection for surrogate training}
\label{alg:data-collection}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
  \Require Number of workers $W = 16$, Sobol sequence $\mathcal{S}$, perturbation fraction $p = 0.3$
  \Ensure Dataset $\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{s}_t, \mathbf{a}_t, \mathbf{s}_{t+1})\}$
  \For{each worker $w = 1, \ldots, W$ in parallel}
    \While{not done}
      \State Initialize rod; with probability $p$, perturb state
      \State Sample action $\mathbf{a}$ from $\mathcal{S}$ (50\%) or uniform (50\%)
      \State Execute one RL step: $\mathbf{s}_{t+1} \gets T(\mathbf{s}_t, \mathbf{a}_t)$
      \State Store $(\mathbf{s}_t, \mathbf{a}_t, \mathbf{s}_{t+1}, t_{\text{start}})$ to batch file
    \EndWhile
  \EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The collection format stores one transition per row: the current state $\mathbf{s}_t$, the action $\mathbf{a}_t$, the next state $\mathbf{s}_{t+1}$, the episode start time $t_{\text{start}}$, and optionally the internal and external forces and torques acting on the rod.
At approximately 2055 bytes per transition including forces, the target dataset size of 10\,GB yields roughly 5 million transition pairs.
A health monitor tracks the output rate of each worker and respawns any worker that stalls after initialization, ensuring sustained throughput across multi-hour collection runs.

\section{Neural Surrogate Model}
\label{sec:surrogate}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{State and Action Representation for Elastica}
\label{sec:surrogate:representation}

The full 124-dimensional rod state vector $\mathbf{s}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{124}$, defined in \cref{tab:state-vector}, serves as the Markov state for both surrogate training and RL.
The 2D projection from the 3D Cosserat state is exact --- $z$-components are identically zero --- so no physical content is lost.

The action vector $\mathbf{a}_t \in [-1,1]^5$ is linearly mapped to the physical CPG parameter ranges defined in \cref{tab:action-vector}:

\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{a}_{\text{phys}} = \frac{1}{2}\bigl[(\mathbf{a}_t + 1) \odot (\mathbf{a}_{\max} - \mathbf{a}_{\min}) + 2\,\mathbf{a}_{\min}\bigr]
  \label{eq:action-mapping}
\end{equation}

\begin{itemize}
  \item $\mathbf{a}_t \in [-1,1]^5$ --- normalized action from the RL policy
  \item $\mathbf{a}_{\max}$, $\mathbf{a}_{\min}$ --- upper and lower bounds of each CPG parameter
  \item $\odot$ --- element-wise multiplication
\end{itemize}
Denormalization to physical units occurs only inside the PyElastica environment; the surrogate receives $\mathbf{a}_t$ directly.

\subsection{Per-Element CPG Phase Encoding}
\label{sec:surrogate:encoding}

The target curvature at element $e$ depends on both the global oscillation phase $2\pi f t$ and the element's arc-length position $s_e$ through the serpenoid wave (\cref{alg:elastica-transition}, line~5).
Elements at opposite ends of the rod can be at opposite phases of the curvature wave at the same instant.
An early version of the surrogate (V1) used a global phase encoding $[\sin(2\pi f t), \cos(2\pi f t)] \in \mathbb{R}^2$ appended once to the input, requiring the model to reconstruct per-element phase offsets implicitly through shared MLP weights.
This imposed a significant learning burden and contributed to poor angular velocity predictions (R$^2 = 0.23$).

The V2 encoding provides the per-element phase explicitly.
For element $e \in \{1, \ldots, N_e\}$, an effective time is computed as:

\begin{equation}
  t_e = \frac{k \cdot s_e}{f}
  \label{eq:effective-time}
\end{equation}

From this, a phase triplet is constructed:

\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\phi}_e
    = \bigl(\sin(2\pi f \cdot t_e),\;
            \cos(2\pi f \cdot t_e),\;
            \kappa_e^{\text{actual}}\bigr)
  \label{eq:per-element-phase}
\end{equation}

The effective time $t_e = k \cdot s_e / f$ incorporates the spatial phase offset at element $e$, where $k$ is the wave number and $s_e$ is the normalized arc-length position.
The sine and cosine terms give the model the exact phase of the serpenoid wave at each element's location, and $\kappa_e^{\text{actual}}$ is the instantaneous curvature providing the control error that drives the elastic restoring force.

Stacking $\boldsymbol{\phi}_e$ for all $N_e = 20$ elements yields a 60-dimensional phase feature vector.
These features are computed on-the-fly during dataset loading from the stored action and episode start time $t_{\text{start}}$, so batch files contain only rod states, actions, and $t_{\text{start}}$ without pre-computed phase data.

The full surrogate input concatenates the z-score normalized rod state $\bar{\mathbf{s}}_t$, the normalized action $\mathbf{a}_t$, and the per-element phase features:

\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{z}_t
    = \bigl[\bar{\mathbf{s}}_t \;\|\; \mathbf{a}_t \;\|\;
            \boldsymbol{\phi}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{\phi}_{N_e}\bigr]
    \in \mathbb{R}^{189}
  \label{eq:surrogate-input}
\end{equation}

\begin{itemize}
  \item $\bar{\mathbf{s}}_t = (\mathbf{s}_t - \boldsymbol{\mu}_s)/\boldsymbol{\sigma}_s$ --- z-score normalized state using training-set per-feature mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}_s$ and standard deviation $\boldsymbol{\sigma}_s$
  \item $\mathbf{a}_t \in [-1,1]^5$ --- normalized CPG action
  \item $\boldsymbol{\phi}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{\phi}_{N_e}$ --- per-element phase triplets (60 dimensions total)
\end{itemize}
The 189 dimensions decompose as $124 + 5 + 60$.

\subsection{Surrogate Model Architecture}
\label{sec:surrogate:architecture}

The surrogate replaces the entire transition operator $T$ with a single forward pass:

\begin{equation}
  \underbrace{\text{Cosserat PDE}}_{\text{continuous}}
    \;\xrightarrow{\;\epsilon_{\text{space}}\;}
  \underbrace{\dot{\mathbf{s}} = \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{a}, t)}_{\text{124-dim ODE}}
    \;\xrightarrow{\;\epsilon_{\text{time}}\;}
  \underbrace{T = V_{\Delta t}^{500}}_{\text{Alg.~\ref{alg:elastica-transition}}}
    \;\xrightarrow{\;\epsilon_{\text{nn}}\;}
  \underbrace{f_\theta}_{\text{surrogate}}
  \label{eq:approximation-chain}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
  f_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t) \approx T(\mathbf{s}_t, \mathbf{a}_t) - \mathbf{s}_t,\quad
  \mathbf{z}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{189},\;
  f_\theta \colon \mathbb{R}^{189} \!\to\! \mathbb{R}^{124}
  \label{eq:nn-approximation}
\end{equation}

\noindent
The surrogate is a feedforward MLP.
It maps the 189-dimensional input~$\mathbf{z}_t$ to a 124-dimensional normalized state delta, from which the predicted next state is recovered through affine reconstruction:\looseness=-1

\begin{equation}
  f_\theta : \mathbf{z}_t \mapsto \overline{\Delta\mathbf{s}}
  \label{eq:surrogate-forward}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathbf{s}}_{t+1}
    = \mathbf{s}_t
      + \boldsymbol{\sigma}_\Delta \odot f_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t)
      + \boldsymbol{\mu}_\Delta
  \label{eq:next-state-reconstruction}
\end{equation}

\begin{itemize}
  \item $f_\theta$ --- neural surrogate with parameters $\theta$
  \item $\overline{\Delta\mathbf{s}}$ --- predicted normalized state delta
  \item $\boldsymbol{\mu}_\Delta, \boldsymbol{\sigma}_\Delta \in \mathbb{R}^{124}$ --- training-set mean and standard deviation of the state delta $(\mathbf{s}_{t+1} - \mathbf{s}_t)$, computed once before training begins
\end{itemize}

Delta prediction offers two advantages over predicting the absolute next state.
First, per-step state changes are small relative to absolute values --- position deltas are typically on the order of centimeters per control step while absolute positions span meters --- so the target distribution is more concentrated and easier to fit.
Second, when $f_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t) \approx \mathbf{0}$, the reconstruction yields $\hat{\mathbf{s}}_{t+1} \approx \mathbf{s}_t + \boldsymbol{\mu}_\Delta$, providing a reasonable default (the mean empirical transition) that acts as an implicit residual connection.

The network architecture is:

\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{R}^{189}
    \xrightarrow{\text{Linear}}
    \bigl[\text{Linear}(512) \to \text{LayerNorm} \to \text{SiLU}\bigr]^{\times 3}
    \xrightarrow{\text{Linear}}
    \mathbb{R}^{124}
  \label{eq:architecture}
\end{equation}

The output layer is initialized to zero so that the model starts by predicting zero delta.
LayerNorm is applied after each linear transformation (before the SiLU activation) to stabilize training dynamics.
The total number of trainable parameters is approximately 659K.

\subsection{Configurations and Hyperparameters}
\label{sec:surrogate:hyperparams}

Training is organized in two phases that introduce the rollout loss gradually to avoid instability during early optimization.

\subsubsection{Phase 1: Single-Step MSE (Epochs 1--20)}
\label{sec:surrogate:training:phase1}

The model is trained to minimize the mean squared error on the normalized state delta:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{single}}
    = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N}
      \bigl\| f_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t^{(i)})
             - \overline{\Delta\mathbf{s}}_{\text{true}}^{(i)} \bigr\|^2
  \label{eq:single-loss}
\end{equation}

The sum runs over $N$ training samples, where $f_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t^{(i)})$ is the predicted normalized delta for sample $i$ and $\overline{\Delta\mathbf{s}}_{\text{true}}^{(i)}$ is the corresponding ground truth.
This phase establishes accurate one-step predictions before the more expensive rollout computation is introduced.

\subsubsection{Phase 2: Combined Loss with Rollout (Epoch 20 Onward)}
\label{sec:surrogate:training:phase2}

An 8-step autoregressive rollout loss is added to penalize error accumulation:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}
    = \mathcal{L}_{\text{single}}
      + \lambda_r \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{rollout}}
  \label{eq:combined-loss}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{rollout}}
    = \frac{1}{L} \sum_{t=0}^{L-1}
      \bigl\| \hat{\mathbf{s}}_{t+1} - \mathbf{s}_{t+1}^{\text{true}} \bigr\|^2
  \label{eq:rollout-loss}
\end{equation}

The rollout weight is $\lambda_r = 0.1$ and the rollout length is $L = 8$ steps.
At each step, the model's own prediction $\hat{\mathbf{s}}_{t+1}$ is used as input for the next, and gradients are back-propagated through the full unrolled trajectory.
This loss directly penalizes the drift between surrogate rollouts and ground-truth PyElastica trajectories --- the primary failure mode when the surrogate is used as a substitute environment for RL training.

\subsubsection{Density-Weighted Sampling}
\label{sec:surrogate:training:density}

Sobol quasi-random action sampling produces a non-uniform distribution over the state space.
To compensate, each training sample is weighted by the inverse of its estimated local density:

\begin{equation}
  w^{(i)} = \frac{1}{\hat{p}(\mathbf{c}^{(i)})}
  \label{eq:density-weight}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{c}^{(i)}
    = \bigl[\bar{x}_{\text{CoM}},\;
            \bar{y}_{\text{CoM}},\;
            \|\dot{\mathbf{x}}\|,\;
            |\bar{\omega}_z|\bigr]
  \label{eq:density-features}
\end{equation}

\begin{itemize}
  \item $w^{(i)}$ --- importance weight for sample $i$
  \item $\hat{p}$ --- estimated local density (joint histogram with 20 bins per dimension)
  \item $\mathbf{c}^{(i)} \in \mathbb{R}^4$ --- summary feature capturing center-of-mass position ($\bar{x}_{\text{CoM}}$, $\bar{y}_{\text{CoM}}$), speed ($\|\dot{\mathbf{x}}\|$), and mean angular velocity magnitude ($|\bar{\omega}_z|$)
\end{itemize}
Weights are clipped at $10\times$ the mean to prevent rare samples from dominating the gradient.

\subsection{Periodic Pattern Learning}
\label{sec:surrogate:periodic}

The CPG's time-varying rest curvature creates a frequency-domain challenge for the surrogate.
During each RL step, the serpenoid wave completes $n_{\text{cycles}} = f \cdot \Delta t_{\text{ctrl}}$ oscillation cycles, and the integrated dynamical response is an oscillatory function of the action parameters $(f, k)$.
Standard MLPs suffer from \emph{spectral bias} --- the tendency to learn low-frequency target components before high-frequency ones --- making the $f \mapsto \Delta\omega_z$ relationship particularly difficult to capture.

The existing per-element phase encoding (\cref{sec:surrogate:encoding}) addresses the \emph{spatial-temporal phase} at each element.
Three additional solutions target the remaining \emph{frequency-domain} gap:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Random Fourier Features on $(f, k)$:}
    Map the normalized frequency and wave number through $\gamma(\mathbf{v}) = [\sin(2\pi \mathbf{B}\mathbf{v}),\; \cos(2\pi \mathbf{B}\mathbf{v})]$ with $\mathbf{B} \in \mathbb{R}^{32 \times 2}$ sampled from $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$.
    This provides sinusoidal basis functions that the MLP would otherwise need to synthesize internally, converting the hard nonlinear approximation into an easier regression.

  \item \textbf{Explicit $n_{\text{cycles}}$ encoding:}
    Two features $[\sin(2\pi n_{\text{cycles}}), \cos(2\pi n_{\text{cycles}})]$ directly encode how many oscillation cycles fit within one RL step.

  \item \textbf{Harmonic phase features:}
    Extend the per-element encoding from the fundamental ($H{=}1$) to $H{=}3$ harmonics: $\sin(h\psi_j)$, $\cos(h\psi_j)$ for $h = 1, 2, 3$, capturing the rod's nonlinear response to sinusoidal forcing.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
Alternative approaches --- SIREN (periodic activations), Snake activations, Neural ODEs, and Fourier Neural Operators --- were considered and rejected: the periodicity is in the \emph{input encoding}, not the \emph{network architecture}, and the surrogate's fixed-step, finite-dimensional structure does not match these approaches' strengths.

\label{sec:surrogate:results}

The validation loss decreases steadily throughout training, with the train--validation gap widening after epoch~40, indicating mild overfitting.

\section{Physics-Informed Surrogate}
\label{sec:pinn}
% ===========================================================================

The data-driven surrogate of \cref{sec:surrogate} treats the simulator as a black box, learning state transitions purely from input--output examples.
An alternative family of methods---\emph{physics-informed neural networks} (PINNs)---embeds the known governing equations directly into the training loss, reducing data requirements and improving physical consistency of predictions.
This section introduces the standard PINN formulation, its domain-decoupled variant (DD-PINN), the implementation developed in this project, and a comparative analysis.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Standard PINN}
\label{sec:pinn:standard}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

Physics-informed neural networks \citep{Raissi2019} augment the purely data-driven loss with a \emph{physics residual} term derived from the governing equations.
Consider the state-space model
\begin{equation}
  \dot{\mathbf{x}}(t) = \fssm\bigl(\mathbf{x}(t),\, \mathbf{u}(t)\bigr), \qquad \mathbf{x}(0) = \mathbf{x}_0,
  \label{eq:pinn:ssm}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x}(t) \in \mathbb{R}^m$ is the state, $\mathbf{u}(t) \in \mathbb{R}^p$ is the input, and $\fssm$ encodes the known (but expensive) physics---for the snake robot, the Cosserat rod equations of \cref{sec:physics:formulation}.
A feedforward network $\mathbf{h}_\theta$ predicts the state at time~$t$:
\begin{equation}
  \xhat_t = \mathbf{h}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_0,\, \mathbf{u},\, t).
  \label{eq:pinn:pred}
\end{equation}
The PINN loss combines three objectives:
\begin{multline}
  \mathcal{L}(\theta)
    = \underbrace{\lambda_{\mathrm{IC}} \bigl\| \xhat_0 - \mathbf{x}_0 \bigr\|^2}_{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{IC}}}
    + \underbrace{\lambda_{\mathrm{phys}} \frac{1}{N_c}\sum_{k=1}^{N_c}
      \left\| \frac{\partial \xhat_{t_k}}{\partial t} - \fssm(\xhat_{t_k}, \mathbf{u}_{t_k}) \right\|^2}_{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{phys}}} \\
    + \underbrace{\lambda_{\mathrm{data}} \frac{1}{N_d}\sum_{k=1}^{N_d}
      \left\| \xhat_{t_k} - \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{data},k} \right\|^2}_{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}}}.
  \label{eq:pinn:loss}
\end{multline}
The physics residual is evaluated at $N_c$ \emph{collocation points}---randomly sampled time instants where the governing equations are enforced without requiring simulation data.
The time derivative $\partial \xhat_t / \partial t$ is obtained via \emph{automatic differentiation} (autodiff) through the network with respect to the input~$t$.

\paragraph{Explicit loss decomposition.}
Each term in the PINN loss serves a distinct role:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textbf{Initial condition loss} $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{IC}}$: penalizes deviation from the known starting state.
    Since the network receives $\mathbf{x}_0$ as input and must reproduce it at $t{=}0$:
    \begin{equation}
      \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{IC}} = \bigl\| \mathbf{h}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_0,\, \mathbf{u},\, 0) - \mathbf{x}_0 \bigr\|^2.
      \label{eq:pinn:ic-explicit}
    \end{equation}
    This is only approximately satisfied via gradient descent---the network can still violate the initial condition.

  \item \textbf{Physics residual loss} $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{phys}}$: enforces the governing ODE at collocation points $\{t_k\}_{k=1}^{N_c}$ sampled over $[0, T_s]$.
    At each collocation point, the residual vector is
    \begin{equation}
      \mathbf{r}(t_k) = \underbrace{\frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_0,\, \mathbf{u},\, t_k)}{\partial t}}_{\text{autodiff through network}} - \underbrace{\fssm\bigl(\mathbf{h}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_0,\, \mathbf{u},\, t_k),\, \mathbf{u}_{t_k}\bigr)}_{\text{physics at predicted state}},
      \label{eq:pinn:residual-explicit}
    \end{equation}
    and the loss aggregates the squared residuals:
    \begin{equation}
      \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{phys}} = \frac{1}{N_c}\sum_{k=1}^{N_c} \bigl\| \mathbf{r}(t_k) \bigr\|^2.
      \label{eq:pinn:phys-explicit}
    \end{equation}
    Computing $\partial \mathbf{h}_\theta / \partial t$ requires backpropagating through the entire network with respect to the input~$t$, distinct from the weight-gradient backpropagation used for optimization---doubling the autodiff cost per collocation point.

  \item \textbf{Data loss} $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}}$: matches predictions against observed simulator trajectories:
    \begin{equation}
      \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}} = \frac{1}{N_d}\sum_{k=1}^{N_d} \bigl\| \mathbf{h}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_0^{(k)},\, \mathbf{u}^{(k)},\, t^{(k)}) - \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{data}}^{(k)} \bigr\|^2.
      \label{eq:pinn:data-explicit}
    \end{equation}
    This term is optional---PINNs can train with $N_d = 0$ if the physics is fully specified.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
The weights $\lambda_{\mathrm{IC}}, \lambda_{\mathrm{phys}}, \lambda_{\mathrm{data}}$ control the relative importance of each objective.
Choosing these weights is non-trivial: if $\lambda_{\mathrm{phys}}$ dominates, the network satisfies the ODE but ignores data; if too small, physics provides no regularization.

While PINNs can train with little or no observed data, three practical bottlenecks limit their scalability:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Expensive autodiff.} Computing $\partial \xhat_t / \partial t$ requires an additional backward pass through the entire computational graph for each collocation point, adding significant cost for large state spaces.\looseness=-1
  \item \textbf{Loss balancing.} The three loss terms ($\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{IC}}$, $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{phys}}$, $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}}$) compete during training and require careful weighting schemes.
  \item \textbf{Training instability.} For systems with many coupled states ($m \gg 1$), PINN predictions can diverge.
\end{enumerate}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{DD-PINN Method}
\label{sec:pinn:ddpinn}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

The \emph{domain-decoupled} PINN (DD-PINN) \citep{Krauss2024,Licher2025} resolves all three bottlenecks through a single architectural change: \textbf{remove time as a network input} and instead let the network output the parameters of an analytically differentiable \emph{ansatz function}.

\paragraph{Core idea.}
The predicted state is decomposed as
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\xhat_t = \mathbf{g}(\avec, t) + \mathbf{x}_0, \qquad \avec = \fnn(\mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u}_0;\, \theta),}
  \label{eq:pinn:ddpinn-core}
\end{equation}
where $\fnn$ is a feedforward network and $\mathbf{g}$ is the ansatz.
The ansatz must satisfy two properties: (i)~differentiable in closed form for all $t \in [0, T_s]$, so $\dot{\mathbf{g}}$ can be computed analytically; and (ii)~$\mathbf{g}(\avec, 0) \equiv \mathbf{0}$, which guarantees $\xhat_0 = \mathbf{x}_0$ exactly.

\paragraph{Ansatz function.}
For each state variable $j \in \{1, \ldots, m\}$, the ansatz is a sum of $n_g$ damped sinusoidal basis functions:
\begin{equation}
  g_j(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{n_g} \alpha_{ij}\, e^{-\delta_{ij} t}
    \bigl[\phig(\beta_{ij}\, t + \gamma_{ij}) - \phig(\gamma_{ij})\bigr],
  \label{eq:pinn:ansatz}
\end{equation}
where $\phig(x) = \sin(x)$ and the subtraction $\phig(\gamma_{ij})$ ensures $g_j(0) = 0$.
The network outputs the parameter vector
$\avec = (\bm{\alpha},\, \bm{\beta},\, \bm{\gamma},\, \bm{\delta}) \in \mathbb{R}^{4 m n_g}$,
with each parameter playing an interpretable role: $\alpha_{ij}$ (amplitude), $\beta_{ij}$ (frequency), $\gamma_{ij}$ (phase offset), $\delta_{ij} \geq 0$ (damping rate).

\paragraph{Closed-form gradients.}
The time derivative is computed analytically:
\begin{equation}
  \dot{g}_j(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{n_g} \alpha_{ij}\, e^{-\delta_{ij} t}
    \bigl[\beta_{ij}\, \cos(\beta_{ij}\, t + \gamma_{ij})
    - \delta_{ij}\, \sin(\beta_{ij}\, t + \gamma_{ij})\bigr].
  \label{eq:pinn:closed-form}
\end{equation}
These are elementary operations---no computational graph is constructed, and the cost scales linearly with $n_g \cdot m$ regardless of network depth.

\paragraph{Simplified loss.}
Since $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{IC}} \equiv 0$ by construction, the training loss reduces to two terms:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \lambda_{\mathrm{phys}}\, \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{phys}}
    + \lambda_{\mathrm{data}}\, \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}},
  \label{eq:pinn:ddpinn-loss}
\end{equation}
where the autodiff-based derivative in the standard PINN is replaced by the closed-form ansatz derivative.

\paragraph{Explicit DD-PINN loss construction.}
The two loss terms are constructed as follows.
The \textbf{physics residual loss} evaluates the ODE mismatch at $N_c$ collocation points $\{t_k\}_{k=1}^{N_c} \subset [0, T_s]$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{phys}}(\theta) = \frac{1}{N_c}\sum_{k=1}^{N_c}
    \left\| \dot{\mathbf{g}}(\avec, t_k) - \fssm\bigl(\mathbf{g}(\avec, t_k) + \mathbf{x}_0,\;\mathbf{u}_{t_k}\bigr) \right\|^2,
  \label{eq:pinn:ddpinn-phys-explicit}
\end{equation}
where $\dot{\mathbf{g}}(\avec, t_k)$ is computed analytically via~\eqref{eq:pinn:closed-form} and $\fssm$ is the differentiable Cosserat rod right-hand side (\cref{sec:pinn:cosserat-rhs}).
The predicted state at each collocation point is $\xhat_{t_k} = \mathbf{g}(\avec, t_k) + \mathbf{x}_0$, and both the ansatz derivative and the physics evaluation are performed in physical units before nondimensionalization (\cref{sec:pinn:nondim}).

The \textbf{data loss} compares the predicted state delta against ground-truth simulator transitions:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}}(\theta) = \frac{1}{N_d}\sum_{k=1}^{N_d}
    \bigl\| \hat{\Delta}\mathbf{x}^{(k)} - \Delta\mathbf{x}^{(k)} \bigr\|^2,
  \label{eq:pinn:ddpinn-data-explicit}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\Delta}\mathbf{x}^{(k)} = \mathbf{g}(\avec^{(k)}, T_s)$ is the predicted delta at the end of the control interval and $\Delta\mathbf{x}^{(k)} = \mathbf{x}_{T_s}^{(k)} - \mathbf{x}_0^{(k)}$ is the ground-truth transition from the simulator dataset.
This loss operates in the normalized relative representation used by the network.

The key difference from the standard PINN is that both $\mathbf{g}$ and $\dot{\mathbf{g}}$ are evaluated without any autodiff---the network $\fnn$ is called \emph{once} to produce $\avec$, and the ansatz evaluates to $N_c$ collocation points and $N_d$ data points using elementary operations.
Because gradient evaluation is cheap, DD-PINN uses 10--25$\times$ more collocation points than standard PINNs, improving physics enforcement.

\paragraph{Inference pipeline.}
\Cref{fig:pinn:architecture} illustrates the DD-PINN inference flow.
The neural network is evaluated \emph{once} per prediction to produce ansatz parameters; the ansatz is then evaluated at arbitrarily many time points without additional network calls.

\subsection{Implementation}
\label{sec:pinn:implementation}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

Three physics-informed approaches were implemented in \texttt{src/pinn/}.
The following subsubsections present the full mathematical formulation of each component.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Differentiable Cosserat Rod Right-Hand Side}
\label{sec:pinn:cosserat-rhs}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

For both the standard PINN and DD-PINN, a differentiable implementation of the Cosserat rod dynamics $\fssm$ is required so that gradients from the physics loss flow back to the network parameters.
This module computes $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \fssm(\mathbf{x})$ for the 124-dimensional state vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{124}$, extending prior PINN work on \emph{static} Cosserat rod approximation \citep{Bensch2024} to the \emph{dynamic} case.
An inextensible rod approximation is used: stretching stiffness is omitted since bending dominates for planar snake locomotion.

The state vector decomposes into six blocks (\cref{tab:state-vector}):
$\mathbf{x} = (x_i,\; y_i,\; \dot{x}_i,\; \dot{y}_i,\; \psi_e,\; \omega_{z,e})$,
with $N_n = 21$ nodes and $N_e = 20$ elements.
The right-hand side computes the time derivative of each block.

\paragraph{Kinematic equations.}
Position and orientation rates follow directly from the velocity and angular velocity states:
\begin{equation}
  \dot{x}_i = v_{x,i}, \qquad
  \dot{y}_i = v_{y,i}, \qquad
  \dot{\psi}_e = \omega_{z,e},
  \label{eq:pinn:kinematics}
\end{equation}
for $i = 0, \ldots, N_n{-}1$ (nodes) and $e = 0, \ldots, N_e{-}1$ (elements).

\paragraph{Elastic bending moments.}
Discrete curvature at internal joint $j$ (connecting elements $e{=}j$ and $e{=}j{+}1$) is
\begin{equation}
  \kappa_j = \frac{\psi_{j+1} - \psi_j}{\Delta\ell}, \qquad j = 0, \ldots, N_e{-}2,
  \label{eq:pinn:curvature}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta\ell = L / N_e$ is the element length.
The bending moment at each joint is
\begin{equation}
  M_j = B\,\kappa_j = EI\,\frac{\psi_{j+1} - \psi_j}{\Delta\ell},
  \label{eq:pinn:moment}
\end{equation}
with $B = EI$ the bending stiffness and $I = \pi r^4 / 4$ the second moment of area.

The elastic torque on each element arises from the gradient of the bending moment:
\begin{equation}
  \tau_e^{\mathrm{elastic}} =
  \begin{cases}
    M_0 / \Delta\ell, & e = 0 \;\;\text{(first element)}, \\[3pt]
    (M_e - M_{e-1}) / \Delta\ell, & 1 \leq e \leq N_e{-}2, \\[3pt]
    -M_{N_e-2} / \Delta\ell, & e = N_e{-}1 \;\;\text{(last element)}.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:pinn:torque}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Anisotropic RFT friction forces.}
Resistive force theory decomposes the velocity of each node into tangential and normal components relative to the local rod tangent, applying different drag coefficients to each.

The element tangent vector is computed with regularized normalization to ensure smooth gradients near zero element length:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{t}_{x,e} = \frac{x_{e+1} - x_e}{\sqrt{(x_{e+1} - x_e)^2 + (y_{e+1} - y_e)^2 + \varepsilon^2}},
  \label{eq:pinn:tangent-reg}
\end{equation}
and similarly for $\hat{t}_{y,e}$, where $\varepsilon = 10^{-6}$ prevents division by zero.
Node tangents are obtained by averaging neighboring element tangents (with boundary nodes using the nearest element tangent) and re-normalizing:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{t}_{x,i}^{\mathrm{node}} =
  \begin{cases}
    \hat{t}_{x,0}, & i = 0, \\
    \tfrac{1}{2}(\hat{t}_{x,i-1} + \hat{t}_{x,i}), & 1 \leq i \leq N_n{-}2, \\
    \hat{t}_{x,N_e-1}, & i = N_n{-}1.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:pinn:node-tangent}
\end{equation}

Velocity decomposition at each node:
\begin{align}
  v_{\parallel,i} &= v_{x,i}\,\hat{t}_{x,i}^{\mathrm{node}} + v_{y,i}\,\hat{t}_{y,i}^{\mathrm{node}},
  \label{eq:pinn:v-proj} \\
  \mathbf{v}_{\mathrm{tan},i} &= v_{\parallel,i}\,\hat{\mathbf{t}}_i^{\mathrm{node}},
  \qquad
  \mathbf{v}_{\mathrm{norm},i} = \mathbf{v}_i - \mathbf{v}_{\mathrm{tan},i}.
  \label{eq:pinn:v-decomp}
\end{align}
The RFT friction force at each node is then:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{%
    F_{x,i}^{\mathrm{fric}} = -c_t\, v_{\mathrm{tan},x,i} - c_n\, v_{\mathrm{norm},x,i}, \qquad
    F_{y,i}^{\mathrm{fric}} = -c_t\, v_{\mathrm{tan},y,i} - c_n\, v_{\mathrm{norm},y,i},
  }
  \label{eq:pinn:rft}
\end{equation}
where $c_t$ and $c_n$ are the tangential and normal drag coefficients.

\paragraph{Elastic bending forces.}
The bending moment gradient generates transverse forces at internal joints.
The normal direction at joint $j$ is the $90^\circ$ rotation of the joint tangent:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{n}_{x,j} = -\hat{t}_{y,j}^{\mathrm{joint}}, \qquad
  \hat{n}_{y,j} = \hat{t}_{x,j}^{\mathrm{joint}},
  \label{eq:pinn:normal}
\end{equation}
where the joint tangent is the average of the two neighboring element tangents.
The elastic force at node $i = j{+}1$ (the node at joint $j$) is:
\begin{equation}
  F_{x,i}^{\mathrm{elastic}} = \frac{M_j\,\hat{n}_{x,j}}{\Delta\ell^2}, \qquad
  F_{y,i}^{\mathrm{elastic}} = \frac{M_j\,\hat{n}_{y,j}}{\Delta\ell^2},
  \label{eq:pinn:elastic-force}
\end{equation}
with $F^{\mathrm{elastic}} = 0$ at the two boundary nodes.

\paragraph{Newton's second law.}
The translational acceleration at each node combines friction and elastic forces:
\begin{equation}
  \dot{v}_{x,i} = \frac{F_{x,i}^{\mathrm{fric}} + F_{x,i}^{\mathrm{elastic}}}{m_i}, \qquad
  \dot{v}_{y,i} = \frac{F_{y,i}^{\mathrm{fric}} + F_{y,i}^{\mathrm{elastic}}}{m_i},
  \label{eq:pinn:newton}
\end{equation}
where $m_i = \rho A \Delta\ell$ for interior nodes and $m_i = \rho A \Delta\ell / 2$ for the two boundary nodes (half-mass lumping).

\paragraph{Angular momentum balance.}
The angular acceleration of each element is driven by the elastic torque:
\begin{equation}
  \dot{\omega}_{z,e} = \frac{\tau_e^{\mathrm{elastic}}}{\rho I \Delta\ell}.
  \label{eq:pinn:angular-momentum}
\end{equation}

\noindent
The complete right-hand side assembles all six blocks:
\begin{equation}
  \fssm(\mathbf{x}) = \bigl(\dot{x}_i,\; \dot{y}_i,\; \dot{v}_{x,i},\; \dot{v}_{y,i},\; \dot{\psi}_e,\; \dot{\omega}_{z,e}\bigr) \in \mathbb{R}^{124}.
  \label{eq:pinn:rhs-full}
\end{equation}

The dominant physics for planar snake locomotion---bending elasticity and anisotropic RFT friction ($c_n / c_t = 10$)---is preserved in the DD-PINN RHS.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Physics-Regularized Surrogate Loss}
\label{sec:pinn:regularizer}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

The simplest physics-informed approach adds algebraic constraints as soft regularizers to the data-driven surrogate of \cref{sec:surrogate}.
Unlike the PINN and DD-PINN, this approach requires \emph{no differentiable simulator}---the constraints are derived from kinematic identities and energy bounds on the 124-dimensional state delta $\Delta\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}_{t+1} - \mathbf{x}_t$.
All constraints use trapezoidal integration for improved accuracy.

\paragraph{Constraint 1: Translational kinematic consistency.}
The predicted position change must be consistent with the predicted velocity change via trapezoidal integration:
\begin{align}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{kin},x} &= \frac{1}{N_n}\sum_{i=0}^{N_n-1}
    \Bigl( \Delta x_i - \bigl(v_{x,i} + \tfrac{1}{2}\,\Delta v_{x,i}\bigr)\,\Delta t \Bigr)^2,
  \label{eq:pinn:reg-kin-x} \\
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{kin},y} &= \frac{1}{N_n}\sum_{i=0}^{N_n-1}
    \Bigl( \Delta y_i - \bigl(v_{y,i} + \tfrac{1}{2}\,\Delta v_{y,i}\bigr)\,\Delta t \Bigr)^2,
  \label{eq:pinn:reg-kin-y}
\end{align}
where $\Delta t = 0.5$\,s is the control timestep, $v_{x,i}$ is the current velocity, and $\Delta v_{x,i}$ is the predicted velocity change.
The trapezoidal form uses the average velocity $\bar{v} = v + \frac{1}{2}\Delta v$ rather than the current velocity alone.

\paragraph{Constraint 2: Angular kinematic consistency.}
Analogously, the predicted yaw change must match the integrated angular velocity:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ang}} = \frac{1}{N_e}\sum_{e=0}^{N_e-1}
    \Bigl( \Delta\psi_e - \bigl(\omega_{z,e} + \tfrac{1}{2}\,\Delta\omega_{z,e}\bigr)\,\Delta t \Bigr)^2.
  \label{eq:pinn:reg-ang}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Constraint 3: Curvature--moment consistency.}
The change in discrete curvature should be consistent with the spatial gradient of the predicted angular velocity change:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{curv}} = 0.1 \cdot \frac{1}{N_e{-}1}\sum_{j=0}^{N_e-2}
    \left( \frac{\psi_{j+1}' - \psi_j'}{\Delta\ell} - \frac{\psi_{j+1} - \psi_j}{\Delta\ell}
    - \frac{\Delta\omega_{z,j+1} - \Delta\omega_{z,j}}{\Delta\ell}\,\Delta t \right)^2,
  \label{eq:pinn:reg-curv}
\end{equation}
where $\psi' = \psi + \Delta\psi$ is the predicted next yaw.
This constraint is weighted at $0.1\times$ because the curvature--moment relation is approximate for large deformations.

\paragraph{Constraint 4: Bounded kinetic energy change.}
The kinetic energy change between consecutive states should not exceed a threshold $E_{\mathrm{thr}}$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{energy}} = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B}
    \Bigl[\operatorname{ReLU}\bigl(|\Delta\mathrm{KE}_b| - E_{\mathrm{thr}}\bigr)\Bigr]^2,
  \label{eq:pinn:reg-energy}
\end{equation}
where the kinetic energy and its change are:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{KE} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=0}^{N_n-1} (v_{x,i}^2 + v_{y,i}^2), \qquad
  \Delta\mathrm{KE} = \mathrm{KE}' - \mathrm{KE}.
  \label{eq:pinn:ke}
\end{equation}
The ReLU activation ensures this constraint is only active when the energy change is unreasonably large ($|\Delta\mathrm{KE}| > E_{\mathrm{thr}}$), acting as a one-sided penalty.

\paragraph{Total regularization loss.}
The four constraints sum to form the physics regularization:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{%
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{reg}} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{kin},x} + \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{kin},y} + \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ang}} + \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{curv}} + \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{energy}}.
  }
  \label{eq:pinn:reg-total}
\end{equation}

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{DD-PINN Ansatz and Model}
\label{sec:pinn:ansatz-impl}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

The \texttt{DampedSinusoidalAnsatz} implements \cref{eq:pinn:ansatz} with softplus-enforced positive damping coefficients.
The network $\fnn$ outputs a flat parameter vector which is reshaped and parsed as:
\begin{equation}
  \avec = \fnn(\mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{u};\,\theta) \in \mathbb{R}^{4 m n_g},
  \qquad
  \avec \;\mapsto\;
  \underbrace{\alpha_{ij}}_{\text{amplitude}},\;
  \underbrace{\delta_{ij} = \operatorname{softplus}(\tilde{\delta}_{ij})}_{\text{damping} \geq 0},\;
  \underbrace{\beta_{ij}}_{\text{frequency}},\;
  \underbrace{\gamma_{ij}}_{\text{phase}},
  \label{eq:pinn:param-parse}
\end{equation}
for $i = 1, \ldots, n_g$ (basis functions) and $j = 1, \ldots, m$ (state dimensions), where $\operatorname{softplus}(x) = \ln(1 + e^x)$ ensures non-negative damping.

The \texttt{DDPINNModel} wraps the feedforward network with the ansatz.
A \texttt{FourierFeatureEmbedding} layer mitigates spectral bias by projecting inputs through random Fourier features before the hidden layers.
The output layer is zero-initialized so that the initial prediction is zero displacement ($\xhat_t = \mathbf{x}_0$).
For the 130-dimensional relative state with $n_g = 5$ basis functions, the network output dimension is $4 \times 130 \times 5 = 2{,}600$.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Nondimensionalization}
\label{sec:pinn:nondim}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

The physics residual loss involves quantities spanning vastly different scales---positions in meters, velocities in m/s, angular velocities in rad/s, and forces in newtons.
Without normalization, the loss would be dominated by whichever component has the largest magnitude.
All quantities are nondimensionalized using physics-based reference scales rather than statistical z-score normalization, ensuring the physics residual remains meaningful:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    L_{\mathrm{ref}} &= L = 1.0\;\text{m} && \text{(rod length)}, \\
    t_{\mathrm{ref}} &= \Delta t_{\mathrm{ctrl}} = 0.5\;\text{s} && \text{(control timestep)}, \\
    V_{\mathrm{ref}} &= L_{\mathrm{ref}} / t_{\mathrm{ref}} = 2.0\;\text{m/s} && \text{(characteristic velocity)}, \\
    \omega_{\mathrm{ref}} &= 1 / t_{\mathrm{ref}} = 2.0\;\text{rad/s} && \text{(characteristic angular velocity)}, \\
    F_{\mathrm{ref}} &= EI / L_{\mathrm{ref}}^2 && \text{(characteristic elastic force)},
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:pinn:nondim-scales}
\end{equation}
where $I = \pi r^4 / 4$ is the second moment of area.
The nondimensionalization applies component-wise to the 124-dimensional state:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{x}_i = \frac{x_i}{L_{\mathrm{ref}}}, \qquad
  \tilde{v}_{x,i} = \frac{v_{x,i}}{V_{\mathrm{ref}}}, \qquad
  \tilde{\psi}_e = \psi_e, \qquad
  \tilde{\omega}_{z,e} = \frac{\omega_{z,e}}{\omega_{\mathrm{ref}}},
  \label{eq:pinn:nondim-state}
\end{equation}
and analogously for $y$-components and the state delta $\Delta\mathbf{x}$.
Yaw angles are already dimensionless (radians) and require no scaling.
The same scaling is applied to the ansatz time derivative $\dot{\mathbf{g}}$ and the Cosserat RHS output $\fssm(\mathbf{x})$ before computing the physics MSE, keeping both sides at $\mathcal{O}(1)$ magnitude.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{ReLoBRaLo Adaptive Loss Weighting}
\label{sec:pinn:relobralo}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

The two-term DD-PINN loss (and the multi-term regularized loss) requires balancing competing objectives.
ReLoBRaLo \citep{Bischof2022} adjusts weights based on the relative rate of change of each loss term.

At iteration $n$, given $K$ loss terms $\ell_1^{(n)}, \ldots, \ell_K^{(n)}$, the algorithm:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Selects a reference loss vector: with probability $\rho$ use the previous iteration $\bm{\ell}^{(n-1)}$, otherwise use a random lookback $\bm{\ell}^{(n-2)}$.
  \item Computes relative change ratios:
    \begin{equation}
      r_k = \frac{\ell_k^{(n)}}{\ell_k^{\mathrm{ref}} + \epsilon}, \qquad k = 1, \ldots, K.
      \label{eq:pinn:relobralo-ratio}
    \end{equation}
  \item Applies temperature-scaled softmax to produce unnormalized weights:
    \begin{equation}
      \hat{w}_k = K \cdot \operatorname{softmax}\!\left(\frac{r_k}{\tau}\right) = K \cdot \frac{\exp(r_k / \tau)}{\sum_{j=1}^{K} \exp(r_j / \tau)}.
      \label{eq:pinn:relobralo-softmax}
    \end{equation}
  \item Smooths with an exponential moving average:
    \begin{equation}
      w_k^{(n)} = \alpha\, w_k^{(n-1)} + (1 - \alpha)\,\hat{w}_k.
      \label{eq:pinn:relobralo-ema}
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

The hyperparameters are: $\alpha = 0.999$ (heavy EMA smoothing), $\tau = 1.0$ (softmax temperature), and $\rho = 0.999$ (lookback probability).
The resulting weights $w_{\mathrm{data}}, w_{\mathrm{phys}}$ upweight whichever loss is decreasing more slowly, preventing any single objective from dominating.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Training Loss Construction}
\label{sec:pinn:loss-construction}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

\paragraph{DD-PINN total loss.}
Combining the data loss, physics loss, adaptive weights, and curriculum warmup, the DD-PINN training loss at epoch $n$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{%
    \mathcal{L}^{(n)}(\theta) = w_{\mathrm{data}}^{(n)}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}}(\theta)
    + w_{\mathrm{phys}}^{(n)}\,\lambda\,\sigma(n)\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{phys}}(\theta),
  }
  \label{eq:pinn:ddpinn-total}
\end{equation}
where $w_{\mathrm{data}}^{(n)}, w_{\mathrm{phys}}^{(n)}$ are the ReLoBRaLo weights, $\lambda$ is a fixed physics strength hyperparameter, and $\sigma(n)$ is the curriculum schedule:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma(n) =
  \begin{cases}
    0, & n < 0.15\,N_{\mathrm{epochs}} \quad\text{(data-only warmup)}, \\[3pt]
    \dfrac{n - 0.15\,N_{\mathrm{epochs}}}{0.30\,N_{\mathrm{epochs}}}, & 0.15\,N_{\mathrm{epochs}} \leq n < 0.45\,N_{\mathrm{epochs}} \quad\text{(linear ramp)}, \\[6pt]
    1, & n \geq 0.45\,N_{\mathrm{epochs}} \quad\text{(full physics)}.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:pinn:curriculum}
\end{equation}
The warmup allows the network to learn a reasonable data-driven initialization before the physics loss activates.

\paragraph{Physics-regularized total loss.}
For the regularized surrogate variant:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}^{(n)}(\theta) = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{data}}(\theta) + \lambda\,\sigma_{\mathrm{reg}}(n)\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{reg}}(\theta),
  \label{eq:pinn:reg-total-loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{reg}}$ is the sum of four algebraic constraints (\cref{eq:pinn:reg-total}) and $\sigma_{\mathrm{reg}}$ follows the same piecewise-linear schedule with a 20\% warmup fraction.
A sweep over $\lambda \in \{0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 1.0\}$ determined the optimal regularization strength.

\paragraph{Coordinate space pipeline.}
The DD-PINN training step requires careful coordinate transformations.
The model operates on a 130-dimensional relative representation (node positions minus center-of-mass, node velocities minus CoM velocity), while the physics loss requires 124-dimensional absolute coordinates because RFT friction forces depend on absolute node velocities through the medium.
Each training step follows the pipeline:
\begin{equation}
  \underbrace{\fnn(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_0^{\mathrm{rel}}, \mathbf{u})}_{\text{network forward}}
  \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{ansatz}\;}
  \underbrace{\mathbf{g},\, \dot{\mathbf{g}}}_{\text{at } N_c \text{ points}}
  \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{denorm}\;}
  \underbrace{\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{phys}}}_{\text{physical units}}
  \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{rel}\to\text{abs}\;}
  \underbrace{\hat{\mathbf{x}}(t_k)}_{\text{124-dim}}
  \;\xrightarrow{\;\fssm\;}
  \underbrace{\dot{\mathbf{x}}_{\mathrm{phys}}}_{\text{RHS}}
  \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{nondim}\;}
  \underbrace{\tilde{\dot{\mathbf{x}}}}_{\mathcal{O}(1)}.
  \label{eq:pinn:pipeline}
\end{equation}
The physics MSE is computed in the nondimensionalized space, where both the ansatz derivative $\dot{\mathbf{g}}$ and the Cosserat RHS output $\fssm(\hat{\mathbf{x}})$ are at comparable magnitudes.

\paragraph{Collocation strategy.}
Temporal collocation points are sampled using Sobol quasi-random sequences for uniform coverage of the prediction interval $[0, \Delta t_{\mathrm{ctrl}}]$, with residual-based adaptive refinement (RAR) concentrating additional points where the physics residual is largest (every 20 epochs, adding 10\% new points).\looseness=-1

\subsection{Direct Simulation Training}
\label{sec:rl:direct}

Direct RL training uses the physics simulator as the environment.
At each step, the RL agent selects CPG parameters $\mathbf{a}_t \in [-1,1]^5$, the simulator advances the rod state via the transition operator $T$ (500 substeps for Elastica, 1 step for DisMech), and a reward signal is computed from the resulting state.
PPO \citep{Janner2019} is used as the policy optimization algorithm with TorchRL as the training framework.

The reward is based on forward displacement potential:
\begin{equation}
  r_t = x_{\text{CoM}}(t+1) - x_{\text{CoM}}(t),
  \label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
where $x_{\text{CoM}}$ is the center-of-mass $x$-coordinate of the rod.
This potential-based reward avoids the noise issues encountered with velocity-based rewards (\cref{tab:issues-training}).

\subsubsection{Hyperparameters}
\label{sec:rl:direct:hyperparams}

\Cref{tab:ppo-hyperparams} summarizes the PPO hyperparameters.

The best results (Session~10) used conservative settings: a lower learning rate ($10^{-4}$ vs.\ $3 \times 10^{-4}$) and fewer PPO epochs per batch (4 vs.\ 10) compared to earlier sessions, which prevented the entropy collapse observed in Session~9.

\Cref{fig:rl-training} shows the 10-batch rolling average reward over 2M training steps.
Training exhibits three distinct phases:
(1)~a \emph{learning phase} (0--164k steps) where rewards rise rapidly to a peak of 140.0;
(2)~a \emph{decline phase} (164k--574k steps) where the policy temporarily degrades, likely due to policy updates overshooting in the CPG parameter space; and
(3)~a \emph{steady state} (574k--2M steps) where the rolling average stabilizes around 102.
The best checkpoint (reward~$= 156.66$) was saved at step~155,648 during the peak.

Compared to Session~9 (aggressive hyperparameters: $\text{lr} = 3 \times 10^{-4}$, 10 PPO epochs), Session~10 achieved a 17\% higher peak (140.0 vs.\ 119.5 rolling average) and maintained healthy entropy throughout training, whereas Session~9 suffered entropy collapse and reward decline after 200k steps.

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
The best checkpoint was evaluated over 20 episodes with deterministic actions and randomized initial headings (\cref{fig:rl-eval}).
The policy produces reliable serpentine locomotion with a mean displacement of $1.495 \pm 0.13$\,m per 500-step episode, corresponding to a speed of ${\sim}0.003$\,m/step ($0.006$\,m/s at $\Delta t = 0.5$\,s per RL step).
Locomotion is consistent across all heading directions, confirming that the policy generalizes across orientations.

No episodes reached the 2.0\,m goal: at the observed speed, the snake requires ${\sim}667$ steps to cover 2.0\,m, exceeding the 500-step episode limit (75\% completion).
The 2.4$\times$ gap between training reward (156.66) and evaluation reward ($64.90 \pm 1.44$) is attributed to
(i)~stochastic actions during training providing exploration bonuses,
(ii)~favorable heading distributions in some training episodes, and
(iii)~goal-reaching bonuses that inflate training-time rewards.

\subsection{Surrogate Environment Training}
\label{sec:rl:surrogate}

The trained neural surrogate serves as a drop-in replacement for the physics simulator during RL training.
The surrogate environment uses $f_\theta$ to compute state transitions, with per-element phase encoding computed on-the-fly from the current action and simulation time.
The projected speedup of $4$--$5$ orders of magnitude enables large-scale hyperparameter sweeps and multi-seed experiments that are infeasible with direct simulation.

\subsubsection{Results}
\label{sec:rl:surrogate:results}

Surrogate-based RL training has not yet been conducted.
While the surrogate model achieves reasonable single-step prediction accuracy for geometric state components (\cref{sec:surrogate:results}), the moderate overall $R^2 = 0.393$ and the poor velocity prediction ($R^2 = 0.145$) raise concerns about multi-step rollout fidelity.
Training an RL policy inside the surrogate environment is planned as the next step; the key risk is that the policy may exploit surrogate artifacts (particularly in $\omega_z$ and velocity predictions) rather than learning physically grounded gaits that transfer back to the Elastica simulator.

\subsection{Challenges}
\label{sec:rl:challenges}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Computational cost.}
    Each Elastica environment step costs ${\sim}46$\,ms.
    With 16 parallel workers, throughput is ${\sim}350$ steps/s.
    A PPO run of $2 \times 10^6$ steps requires ${\sim}36$ hours.
    DisMech is ${\sim}4\times$ faster at ${\sim}12$\,ms/step, but still CPU-bound.

  \item \textbf{Exploration in CPG space.}
    The 5-dimensional CPG action space contains large regions that produce no locomotion (e.g., zero amplitude, extreme frequencies).
    Random exploration is inefficient; curriculum learning over CPG parameter subsets improves sample efficiency.

  \item \textbf{Reward sparsity.}
    Distance-based rewards require the agent to discover forward-moving gaits before receiving meaningful signal.
    Initial policies produce near-zero displacement, making early training gradients uninformative.

  \item \textbf{Backend discrepancy.}
    Policies trained on DisMech do not transfer directly to Elastica due to differences in friction modeling (viscous damping vs.\ RFT), curvature control mechanism (hard constraint vs.\ elastic rest shape), and temporal resolution (1 vs.\ 500 curvature updates per step).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Physics Calibration Challenges}
\label{sec:discussion:physics}

Establishing a reliably locomoting Cosserat rod simulation required resolving several categories of physics calibration errors.
Each issue independently prevented stable serpentine locomotion and would have rendered any surrogate trained on the resulting data physically meaningless.
\Cref{tab:discussion-physics} summarizes the three critical calibration challenges and their resolutions.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \small
  \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.35}
  \caption{Physics calibration challenges. Each independently prevented locomotion.}
  \label{tab:discussion-physics}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}p{2.5cm}p{4.5cm}p{3.0cm}p{3.5cm}@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Challenge} & \textbf{Symptom} & \textbf{Root cause} & \textbf{Resolution} \\
    \midrule
    Rod radius \& bending stiffness
      & $r{=}0.001$\,m: rod limp, zero velocity. $E{\geq}10^6$\,Pa: curvature $\pm100$\,rad/m, velocities $>178$\,m/s, divergence.
      & $EI \propto r^4$; initial $EI \approx 1.6{\times}10^{-6}$\,N$\cdot$m$^2$ insufficient for deformation
      & Grid search: $r{=}0.02$\,m, $E{=}10^5$\,Pa ($I$ increases $160{,}000\times$) \\
    \addlinespace
    Serpenoid wave sign error
      & Consistent negative forward-velocity rewards across all training runs
      & $-\omega t$ instead of $+\omega t$: wave propagates tail-to-head, opposite to required direction for RFT propulsion
      & Fixed to $\kappa = A\sin(ks + \omega t + \phi)$; phase velocity $-f/k$ (head-to-tail) \\
    \addlinespace
    RFT friction ratio
      & Excessive lateral drift; unstable forward propulsion
      & Literature values ($c_n/c_t {\approx} 10$) calibrated for soil, not flat surface
      & Tuned to 5:1 ($c_t{=}0.01$, $c_n{=}0.05$); 94:1 forward/lateral velocity ratio \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent
\textit{Lesson:} Simulator parameter values need not match the physical system's material parameters.
The sign error motivates unit tests that verify wave propagation direction, rather than relying on training metrics to surface physics errors.
